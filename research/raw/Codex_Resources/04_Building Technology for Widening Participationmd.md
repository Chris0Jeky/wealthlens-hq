# Building Technology for Widening Participation

## Executive summary

The UK widening participation landscape is unusually rich in public data, but poor in joined-up, student-friendly products. The core public layers already exist: DfE publishes progression to higher education by free school meal status, disadvantage, ethnicity, SEND, care experience and POLAR; the OfS publishes provider-level access, continuation, completion, attainment and progression data plus postcode lookups for POLAR4 and TUNDRA; UCAS publishes entry-rate and provider data, a composite Multiple Equality Measure, contextual-admissions guidance, a new scholarships tool and adviser-facing outreach tools; Discover Uni publishes course-level earnings and employment data; and the Social Mobility Commission now publishes socio-economic-background data on HE entry, earnings and returns to education. The problem is not absence of evidence. It is that the evidence is fragmented across dashboards, PDFs, CSVs, provider tools and analyst-facing products. ## The public data landscape

For England, the Department for EducationŌĆÖs **Widening participation in higher education** release is one of the most useful public sources for access work. It tracks progression to HE by age 19 for state-funded pupils using matched National Pupil Database, ILR and HESA data, and publishes breakdowns by FSM, disadvantage, gender, ethnicity, SEND, first language, children in need, looked-after children, care leavers, local authority, region, school type and POLAR, including progression to high-tariff providers. Its methodology is also important because it makes clear what the measure does **not** show: it only tracks entry by age 19, does not account for prior attainment, and should be treated as an estimate because of matching procedures. ## What POLAR4 and TUNDRA do well and where they fail

POLAR4 is simple and broadly understood across the sector. It classifies UK small areas into quintiles based on the proportion of young people who enter HE at age 18 or 19, and it remains useful because it is UK-wide and historically embedded in admissions, outreach and provider analysis. Its main strengths are comparability and familiarity. Its main weakness is age and coarseness: the current POLAR4 classification is built using entrants from 2009-10 to 2013-14 and 2011 statistical geographies, so it is not a real-time measure of current neighbourhood opportunity. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view1Ņłü

TUNDRA was created to improve on that. The OfS describes it as an enhanced area-based measure that uses individualised tracking from key stage 4 to HE and restricts the population to pupils in English state-funded mainstream schools. That restriction is exactly why many WP practitioners prefer it operationally: it can reveal disadvantaged state-school pockets that can be hidden inside broader neighbourhood averages. The OfS now explicitly recommends using TUNDRA wherever possible when an area-based participation measure is needed, while still acknowledging that TUNDRA is not a direct replacement for POLAR in every context. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view0Ņłéturn3search10Ņłü

The crucial limitation is conceptual. OfS guidance and FAQs make clear that POLAR and TUNDRA are measures of the **likelihood of participation in HE**, not measures of individual socio-economic disadvantage. A postcode in POLAR or TUNDRA quintile 1 does not automatically mean a given young person is poor, and a young person in quintile 5 may still face substantial disadvantage. That is why UCAS created MEM, why HESA is developing SEISA, and why universities increasingly layer postcode flags with FSM, school-level context, care background, refugee status and other applicant-level indicators. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn3search6Ņłéturn30view0Ņłéturn8search0Ņłéturn2search2Ņłéturn2search5Ņłü

The operational mismatch is also important. The OfS postcode tool is useful for staff and for understanding local participation patterns, but the OfS explicitly says students should not use it on its own to determine eligibility for bursaries, contextual offers or other support. Yet universities do in practice send students there, and some scholarships on UCAS explicitly tell applicants to use contextual-postcode tools. That creates a genuine user-experience problem: the data exists, the sector uses it, but the public tool is not designed to turn it into clear, safe student guidance. ## What tools exist and what is missing

There are now several useful public tools, but they cover different parts of the decision journey. The OfS postcode lookup shows POLAR/TUNDRA/Adult HE; Discover Uni lets students compare courses and see earnings, employment and student-experience data; UCAS shows entry requirements and contextual-admissions guidance; and from December 2025 UCAS also offers a scholarships, bursaries and grants search. In parallel, there are adviser- and provider-facing tools such as UCAS Outreach Connection Service, the UCAS Outreach Evaluator and HEAT. ## How WP data connects to inequality and WealthLens UK

The access story only becomes politically and strategically powerful when it is connected to earnings and wealth. The Social Mobility CommissionŌĆÖs latest public figures show large socio-economic-background gaps in HE entry: in 2024, 45.6 per cent of 18- to 20-year-olds from higher professional backgrounds were enrolled in HE, compared with 25.2 per cent from lower working-class backgrounds. The same data series shows that the gap has narrowed over time, but it remains large. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn31view2Ņłü

The labour-market story does not erase that inequality. The SMCŌĆÖs returns analysis shows that, controlling for socio-economic background, sex and age, first degrees are associated with earnings around 27 per cent above the baseline group, and the SMCŌĆÖs direct-effect analysis shows that even when you hold qualifications constant, 25- to 29-year-olds from higher professional backgrounds still earn 19 per cent more than those from lower working-class backgrounds. In other words: education helps, but it does not fully neutralise class-origin effects. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn31view3Ņłéturn32search0Ņłü

The same pattern appears in DfE graduate outcomes. Sector-level LEO data shows that first-degree graduates from FSM families have lower median earnings than non-FSM peers and are almost twice as likely to have no sustained destination five years after graduation. IFS research summarised on its HE pages adds an important nuance: disadvantaged students may see some of the largest financial benefits from going to university, but they are less likely to attend, and even among students with the same grades on the same HE programme, they still earn less on average later on. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn15view0Ņłéturn21search12Ņłü

That is why a **pipeline visualisation** is not just good communications; it is analytically defensible. You can show, on one screen, how childhood disadvantage and geography feed into attainment gaps, how those feed into HE participation and high-tariff participation, how course and provider choices affect early-career outcomes, and how those outcomes sit inside a much wider wealth distribution where the top 1 per cent of households hold the same share of wealth as the bottom 50 per cent combined. ONS reports median household wealth in Great Britain at ┬Ż293,700 for April 2020 to March 2022, with a wealth Gini of 0.59 versus 0.36 for disposable income. That is precisely the frame in which WP stops looking like a discrete outreach programme and starts looking like part of national economic infrastructure. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn20view0Ņłéturn20view1Ņłéturn22view2Ņłü

This is also where a **WealthLens UK** connection becomes strong rather than cosmetic. The same geographic backbone can serve both products: postcode to LSOA/MSOA to local authority to region; deprivation and participation indicators; local earnings and wealth context; and eventually a linked narrative about how education pathways mediate long-run inequality. ## Concrete project proposal

### Project concept

**WP Access Navigator** would be a dual-facing product: a student/parent-facing explorer and a practitioner-facing planning and evaluation workspace. The student side answers the practical questions that official tools currently answer only in fragments. The practitioner side reduces the manual work of targeting, briefing, follow-up and evaluation. It should be positioned as a complement to HEAT and UCAS, not a replacement. HEAT already does longitudinal tracking and evaluation well; your product would sit earlier in the workflow and make outreach more legible, more personalised and easier to measure. ### What the student side should do

A student enters a postcode and sees: their areaŌĆÖs TUNDRA and POLAR position; a short explanation of what those measures mean and do **not** mean; your universityŌĆÖs outreach and contextual-support routes; funding options from your university first, then selected external or UCAS-linked bursary opportunities; and a subject explorer that links likely study options to outcome data from Discover Uni and LEO. The point is not to tell the student ŌĆ£you should do XŌĆØ; it is to replace scattered browsing with one coherent decision surface. That directly addresses the current split between OfS postcode lookup, Discover Uni outcomes and separate bursary search. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn34view3Ņłéturn37view1Ņłéturn37view2Ņłéturn37view3Ņłéturn38view1Ņłéturn38view2Ņłü

One important design choice: do **not** start with a fully personalised ŌĆ£low-income ROI rankingŌĆØ as the flagship feature. Public data is not good enough for that yet. Provider-level LEO does not expose FSM, and DfE warns that the published provider outcomes are raw results that do not control for all underlying differences between students. ### What the practitioner side should do

For WP staff, the tool should support three concrete jobs. First, **targeting**: upload a school list, workshop list or postcode list and instantly enrich it with TUNDRA, POLAR, FSM-area proxies, local progression rates, and existing institutional engagement history. Second, **delivery support**: generate workshop handouts or follow-up links tailored to a school or cohort, including bursaries, contextual-admissions guidance and course examples. Third, **evaluation**: log activity exposure, collect pre/post measures aligned to TASO outcomes frameworks, and export a clean dataset for HEAT or internal analysis. This is where your engineering skill is especially valuable because it converts multiple external datasets into one operational layer. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view2Ņłéturn23search9Ņłéturn5search16Ņłéturn33view3Ņłü

### Data sources and architecture

The most realistic initial stack is straightforward and well matched to your skills: a Python ETL layer for OfS postcode data, DfE widening-participation tables, Discover Uni/LEO extracts and your institutionŌĆÖs bursary/outreach data; FastAPI for a typed internal API; PostgreSQL with PostGIS for geographies; and Vue 3 for the front end. The data model should be geography-first, using postcode to LSOA/MSOA/local-authority mappings so that the same base can later feed WealthLens UK. For evaluation, structure events around intervention, cohort, exposure and outcome entities so that HEAT export is possible from the start. The public side should be anonymous-by-default, with no need to create an account just to explore postcode-based information. The internal side should use university SSO. ### A realistic first-year scope

An MVP should be deliberately narrow. In term one, build the postcode-and-funding explorer for your own institution only, plus a cohort upload screen for staff. In term two, add subject and outcomes comparisons using Discover Uni and LEO, alongside workshop follow-up pages that can be shared with schools. In term three, add evaluation surveys, HEAT export and a light-touch experiment layer for follow-up nudges. The reason to include a nudging module is that evidence from digital messaging shows lower-cost technology interventions can support complex application and transition steps, especially where students have limited access to other support. The internal pitch should not promise a vague ŌĆ£better user experienceŌĆØ. It should propose measured gains in outreach effectiveness. ## Open questions and limitations

The main unresolved issue is **destination modelling**. I did not find a public official dataset that cleanly powers a postcode-to-ŌĆ£universities people from your area usually attendŌĆØ recommender at the granularity a student would expect. A second limitation is **low-income ROI precision**. Public sources let you compare provider/subject outcomes, and they let you compare FSM versus non-FSM outcomes at sector level, but they do not currently provide a public provider-by-subject-by-FSM returns product. A third limitation is **coverage mismatch**.
