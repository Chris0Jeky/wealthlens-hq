## How to use this curriculum

If your goal is to sound like an independent analyst rather than a partisan amplifier, the right order is not ÔÇťopinion first, evidence laterÔÇŁ. It is: measurement, then institutions, then policy, then rhetoric. In the British case, a lot of bad argument comes from collapsing together very different concepts: market income, disposable income, pre-tax national income, post-tax income, income before housing costs, and wealth. It also comes from treating survey wealth data as clean when the UKÔÇÖs main wealth survey lost accredited status in June 2025, and when top-tail estimates move materially depending on whether you rely on surveys alone or combine them with administrative data. The headline Gini can suggest relative stability, but that hides major changes in housing wealth, top incomes, intergenerational chances, and the distributional effects of taxes, transfers, and housing costs. ## The quantitative foundations

With your CS and maths background, you do **not** need a full undergraduate economics retraining programme. You do need the subset of economics that lets you read inequality research without bluffing: welfare economics, public finance, labour economics, urban/housing economics, macro-finance, and the parts of development economics that deal with mobility and state capacity. On the quantitative side, the credibility threshold is: survey design and weighting, equivalisation, quantiles and top shares, index-number thinking, decomposition, causal inference, and the limits of observational data. The decisive skill is not formal cleverness; it is being able to say exactly what a statistic measures, what it omits, and what assumptions sit underneath it. ţłÇciteţłéturn8search3ţłéturn8search11ţłéturn10search2ţłéturn33search2ţłü

The methodological debates you need to know cold are these. First, **Gini versus Palma versus top shares**: the Gini is familiar but can underreact to shifts at the very top or bottom, which is why Palma ratios and top 1% shares are often more politically revealing. Second, **pre-tax versus post-tax versus pre-tax national income**: the choice changes both the level of measured inequality and the policy story you tell about redistribution. Third, **income versus wealth versus consumption**: a country can look middling on disposable-income inequality and still be brutally unequal on housing wealth or inheritances. Fourth, **PPP and cross-country comparability**: international comparisons depend on price-level adjustments and on whether concepts are harmonised. Fifth, **survey versus administrative data**: household surveys often undercount the rich, and in the UK the wealth-data problem is severe enough that the statistics regulator suspended accreditation of the main survey in 2025. Sixth, and very British, **before versus after housing costs**: UK poverty and inequality arguments change substantially once you treat rent and mortgage burden as central rather than incidental. ţłÇciteţłéturn8search3ţłéturn8search11ţłéturn8search15ţłéturn10search1ţłéturn10search2ţłéturn10search10ţłéturn33search0ţłéturn33search3ţłéturn33search2ţłéturn38view2ţłéturn25search7ţłü

## The UK policy landscape

As of May 2026, the UK policy menu on taxing wealth is split into four camps. The **Wealth Tax Commission** remains the most serious design exercise: it judged a one-off wealth tax feasible in the UK, while warning that an annual tax is much harder to justify and administer. Its worked examples are still the reference points for public debate: a one-off tax charged at 1% a year for five years above ┬ú500,000 per individual could raise around ┬ú260 billion, while the same design above ┬ú2 million could raise around ┬ú80 billion; by contrast, an annual tax above ┬ú2 million at about 0.6% is presented as an example that could raise around ┬ú10 billion a year, but with higher avoidance, valuation, and liquidity problems. ţłÇciteţłéturn30view0ţłéturn30view1ţłéturn30view3ţłéturn30view4ţłü

**IPPR** argues that the current taxation of wealth worsens regional divides and has proposed taxing income from wealth more like earnings, closing capital-gains loopholes, and using wealth-tax reform as part of regional rebalancing. **Tax Justice UK** and **Patriotic Millionaires UK** have pushed a broader package of reforms, including a 2% levy on wealth above ┬ú10 million and other changes to capital gains, loopholes, and anti-avoidance, framing them as both revenue-raising and inequality-reducing. The **Resolution Foundation** takes a different line: it does not think a new net-wealth tax is the best route, preferring deep reform of capital gains, inheritance, pensions, and especially property taxation. The current view from the ţłÇentityţłé["organization","Institute for Fiscal Studies","uk economics think tank"]ţłü, sharpened by the 2026 Deaton Review outputs, is similar to that last position: if the goal is better taxation of wealth, fixing existing capital taxes is usually easier and cleaner than launching an annual wealth tax from scratch. ţłÇciteţłéturn29view2ţłéturn27search1ţłéturn27search0ţłéturn27search4ţłéturn22search3ţłéturn22search10ţłéturn28search2ţłéturn28search15ţłéturn27search10ţłéturn12search0ţłéturn31view0ţłü

The history of British wealth-related taxation is politically instructive. The **poll tax** ÔÇö the Community Charge ÔÇö came into force in Scotland in 1989 and in England and Wales in 1990, and was replaced by Council Tax in 1993 after intense opposition. The **bedroom tax**, more officially the removal of the spare room subsidy, has reduced Housing Benefit entitlement for affected social tenants since 1 April 2013. The old **non-dom** remittance basis was abolished from 6 April 2025 and replaced by a four-year foreign-income-and-gains regime for qualifying arrivals after ten years of non-UK residence, alongside wider changes to trusts and transitional rules. And **Capital Gains Tax** rates for most non-property assets rose on 30 October 2024, from 10% and 20% to 18% and 24%. Each of these episodes matters because they show a different failure mode: head taxes can be seen as flatly unfair; housing-related cuts can be politically toxic when they hit people with little room to adjust; elite-focused reforms produce complexity and transitional carve-outs; and capital-tax reform is easier to sell when it looks like base-cleaning rather than confiscation. ţłÇciteţłéturn26search5ţłéturn26search4ţłéturn29view8ţłéturn25search2ţłéturn29view6ţłéturn29view7ţłü

The strongest counterarguments to wealth taxation are not stupid and you should know them well. The big ones are: valuation difficulty, liquidity problems for asset-rich cash-poor households, capital flight and migration, administrative cost, distortion of saving/investment, and the claim that taxing wealth is really double-taxing already-taxed income. Proponents respond in three ways. First, they distinguish **one-off** from **annual** taxes, arguing the former is much less distortive because it taxes past wealth rather than future behaviour. Second, they argue that a broad base, instalment plans, and deferral mechanisms can mitigate liquidity issues. Third, many now concede part of the criticÔÇÖs case and shift toward a more practical agenda: reform capital gains, inheritance, pensions, and land/property taxes first, and reserve a wealth tax for exceptional circumstances or for explicitly redistributionist goals. That is where the UK expert centre of gravity sits today. ţłÇciteţłéturn30view3ţłéturn31view1ţłéturn31view2ţłéturn37search4ţłü

## History, comparison, and the angle you should make your own

The short historical story of Britain is this. Income inequality is no longer surging the way it did in the 1980s, but Britain remains much more unequal than it was in the 1960s and 1970s, and the 1980s shock was broad, not just a top-end anomaly. Research for the Deaton Review shows that the top 1% kept pulling away, while male employment shocks, expanding self-employment, investment income, and pension incomes all helped reshape the distribution. Since the late 2000s, weak overall income growth has coexisted with a huge run-up in household wealth and housing values. That combination ÔÇö stagnant earnings plus inflated assets ÔÇö is the core of the modern UK inequality regime. ţłÇciteţłéturn38view4ţłéturn39view0ţłéturn38view0ţłéturn38view5ţłü

The intergenerational piece is now impossible to ignore. The data behind the Deaton Review show that cohorts born in the 1970s and 1980s have fallen far behind their predecessors on home ownership, while older cohorts benefited from low rates and large capital gains. Separate IFS work shows inheritances are set to matter more for younger generations as older cohorts hold more wealth and younger cohorts do not enjoy stronger income growth. That means British inequality is increasingly about **wealth transmission**, not just annual wages. ţłÇciteţłéturn39view0ţłéturn32search2ţłéturn32search11ţłü

Internationally, the UK sits in an awkward place. The latest ONS figure puts the disposable-income Gini at 32.9% in financial year ending 2024, and Commons Library summaries of OECD data still place the UK among the higher-inequality countries in Europe, albeit below the United States. Nordic countries remain more equal on disposable-income measures because they combine stronger redistribution with different wage-setting and welfare institutions; Norway still levies a net wealth tax, while Denmark and Sweden abolished theirs and lean more heavily on other taxes, including property taxation. France, meanwhile, abolished its old broad wealth tax and replaced it in 2018 with a tax focused on real-estate wealth, which is one reason France is a useful cautionary case rather than a simple model to copy. On wealth concentration, the UK often appears less extreme than the US in OECD and WID comparisons, but the UK evidence is unusually sensitive to how the top tail is measured, so you should always state the measurement caveat out loud. ţłÇciteţłéturn38view3ţłéturn38view4ţłéturn37search1ţłéturn37search9ţłéturn12search10ţłéturn39view4ţłéturn33search2ţłéturn33search4ţłü

Your unusual edge is obvious and worth cultivating deliberately. Most inequality advocates are either narrative people or econometrics people. Very few can think in terms of **strategic interaction**, **network effects**, **state capacity under incomplete information**, and **path-dependent multi-agent systems**. That is exactly where housing, wealth taxation, and financialisation become analytically interesting. You should develop three signatures. First, a **mechanism-design signature**: what can a tax authority know, value, verify, and enforce? Second, an **agent-based signature**: how do debt, asset-price expectations, inherited wealth, and landlord leverage compound over time? Third, a **game-theoretic signature**: how do elites coordinate around exemptions, narrative framing, capital mobility, and policy sequencing? If you build those well, you will not sound like a YouTube summariser; you will sound like someone adding something the field itself needs.
