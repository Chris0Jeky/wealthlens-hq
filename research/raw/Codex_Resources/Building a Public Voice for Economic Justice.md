## Narrative and criticism

The most effective version of your story is **progression, not redemption**. The structure is:

1. **Constraint** — you did not come from privilege.
2. **Craft** — engineering gave you a way to understand and build systems.
3. **Contradiction** — working close to capital-efficient systems sharpened your view of what societies choose to optimise.
4. **Turn** — you decided to use those same skills to make public systems more legible.
5. **Commitment** — widening participation is not branding garnish; it is one of the design criteria for the work itself.

Told this way, the story is not “look how hard I had it” and not “look how morally enlightened I became.” It is “this is the path that made me care about system design, power, and access.” That is credible, durable, and hard to parody. Guidance on ethical storytelling consistently emphasises dignity, permission, and agency, while tech-justice work has similarly argued for centring lived experience without extracting it for performance. citeturn32search10turn32search13turn32search3

A good short bio for you would sound like this:

> I’m a London-based software engineer and researcher. I’ve built systems in industry and in startup environments, including trading infrastructure. Now I’m focused on public-interest engineering: turning inequality data into explanations, tools, and conversations that more people can actually use. I’m especially interested in widening participation, trustworthy public data, and what engineers can do to make systems fairer without pretending software alone solves structural injustice.

That bio works because it does three things: names your craft, names your subject, names your limit.

The biggest pitfall is **tech-saviour framing**. The literature around technosolutionism and “tech for good” is blunt on this point: tools often reproduce existing power if they are not built with participation, governance, and accountability in mind, and “helping” discourse can itself become paternalistic. A good antidote is to make your work visibly collaborative and visibly limited: show the method, show the source, show who else should be in the room, and say what the artifact can and cannot do. Your role is not “I fix inequality.” Your role is “I reduce opacity, improve public legibility, and build useful infrastructure for people doing the deeper work.” citeturn31search2turn31search5turn31search15turn31search20

There are four wording habits that will keep you out of trouble:

- Replace **“I want to fix”** with **“I want to make visible / test / explain / lower barriers to understanding.”**
- Replace **“underserved communities need”** with **“people already doing this work are blocked by…”**
- Replace **“data proves”** with **“this data suggests / reveals / measures, and here are its limits.”**
- Replace **“technology will solve”** with **“technology can support accountability, participation, and coordination.”**

That small language shift moves you out of saviour theatre and into serious public work. citeturn31search1turn31search4turn32search10

When criticism comes from economists or policy people, your answer should be calm and bounded: **“I’m not competing with economists; I’m working on measurement, legibility, reproducibility, infrastructure, and public explanation.”** When criticism comes from people who think tech cannot solve structural problems, the best answer is agreement with precision: **“Yes — which is why I’m not selling software as salvation. I’m trying to make systems more inspectable and participation less exclusionary.”** That posture is stronger than defensiveness because it shows you already understand the limitation that critics think they have just discovered. HN’s own launch guidance has a useful principle here: cheerful, non-defensive engagement with criticism is often most persuasive to the silent audience. citeturn36view10turn31search5turn31search15

  Example: “What institutions say they measure vs what they actually optimise.”
- **Reading notes in public**  
  One paragraph from a paper/report, plus your engineering translation of it.
- **Talk-in-progress**  
  Turn speaking prep into content, then turn content into speaking opportunities.

If you want one simple content ratio for the 90-day sprint, make it this:
- **50% explanatory**
- **20% build-in-public**
- **20% personal/professional story**
- **10% direct opinion**

That balance keeps the brand substantive.

## Open questions and limitations

A few things are still genuinely less settled than the rest of the research.

The evidence on **Bluesky timing and frequency** is materially thinner than the evidence for LinkedIn, X, Instagram, or TikTok. The strongest conclusion there is strategic rather than numerical: Bluesky currently rewards conversation quality, starter-pack/feed placement, and consistent presence more than brute-force posting volume. The 20:00–22:00 UK suggestion is a sensible test window, not a law of nature. citeturn37view8turn6search2

The opportunity landscape for **2027 London events** is only partially published as of 13 May 2026. State of Open Con’s London 2027 date is public, but many other 2027 CFPs are not open yet, so treat the 2026 rooms as the main near-term targets and use them to build video and social proof. citeturn11search2

For **The Pragmatic Engineer**, public guidance is inconsistent: one page still mentions sending a pitch for a guest article, while the newer About page says guest posts are not accepted. I would therefore not build a distribution plan around it; treat it as a “nice if the topic is unusually relevant” outlet rather than a dependable route. citeturn36view6turn36view7

The main conclusion does not depend on those uncertainties: your best path is to build **an own-domain archive, a LinkedIn authority layer, an X conversation engine, a Bluesky quality-network presence, and a small number of extremely reusable talk/blog ideas**. If you do that consistently for 90 days, you will not just have “content.” You will have the beginnings of a public voice.
