# A Six-Month Curriculum for Becoming Dangerous on UK Wealth Inequality

**The most efficient path to credible, independent expertise on UK wealth inequality runs through six layered tiers: the empirical canon (Piketty, Atkinson, Milanovic), the political-economy framework (Christophers, Mazzucato, Acemoglu), the quantitative toolkit (CORE Econ, Cowell, MHE, WID data), the live UK policy fights (Wealth Tax Commission, CenTax, IPPR), the historical context (Thatcher-to-Reeves), and a sustained ongoing-source diet centred on Resolution Foundation, IFS, and a small set of substacks.** For someone with a game-theory and multi-agent cooperation background, the curriculum below also flags a distinct intellectual lane — mechanism design for wealth-tax valuation, yard-sale models of wealth condensation, and network/coalition analyses of tax politics — where you can plausibly produce *original* contributions rather than merely repeat existing arguments.

The plan assumes ~10 hours per week for 26 weeks (260 hours total). It is deliberately weighted toward public-facing credibility over pure academic depth, but the academic spine is real: by Month 6 you will have read Piketty's *Capital and Ideology*, worked through Cowell on inequality measurement, replicated UK Gini estimates from WID data, internalised the Auten–Splinter vs Piketty–Saez–Zucman dispute, and be able to discuss the Wealth Tax Commission's £260bn revenue estimate with the people who wrote it. Crucially, you will not have read everything in this document — you will have built a working library you can return to selectively for decades.

The defining feature of UK wealth inequality in 2026 is the gap between the **£17 trillion** in household wealth (about 7× GDP) and the roughly **3% of GDP** raised by wealth-related taxes. Every serious UK debate — the abolished non-dom regime, Reeves's CGT rises, the BPR/APR farmer protests, the frozen council-tax bands of 1991, the Wealth Tax Commission's still-unimplemented one-off levy — turns on whether that gap is closed, by whom, and at what political price. Your goal over six months is to understand that fight better than 99% of the people having it publicly.

---

## How to use this curriculum

If your goal is to sound like an independent analyst rather than a partisan amplifier, the correct order is **not** "opinion first, evidence later". It is: **measurement, then institutions, then policy, then rhetoric**. In the British case, much weak argument comes from collapsing very different concepts together: market income, disposable income, pre-tax national income, post-tax national income, income before housing costs, income after housing costs, and wealth. It also comes from treating survey wealth data as clean when the UK's main wealth survey — the ONS Wealth and Assets Survey — lost its accredited official statistics status in June 2025, and when top-tail estimates move materially depending on whether you rely on surveys alone or combine them with administrative data. That is why the curriculum below begins with canon-plus-methods before it moves into tax, housing, financialisation, and technology.

A second framing point matters for credibility in UK debates. The headline Gini can suggest relative stability — the latest ONS figure puts the UK disposable-income Gini at 32.9% in the financial year ending 2024 — but that hides major changes in housing wealth, top incomes, intergenerational chances, and the distributional effects of taxes, transfers, and housing costs. In other words: "inequality has not changed much" is often true for one metric and false for the mechanisms that actually drive politics.

## Tier 1 — The essential canon

These are the seven books and ten papers without which no inequality discussion can proceed seriously. Together they take roughly **70–90 hours** to read attentively. Read in the order given.

### The seven essential books

**Thomas Piketty, *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2014)** is the empirical bedrock. Using two centuries of tax and inheritance data, Piketty documented a U-shape: extreme wealth concentration before 1914, compression by 1914–1975 due to wars and progressive taxation, then a return toward Belle Époque levels. His framing of **r > g** as a structural driver remains contested — Acemoglu and Robinson (*JEP* 2015) argue institutions not parameters drive inequality, Rognlie (2015) shows much of capital's rising share is housing, and Mankiw demonstrates that with depreciation and bequest division, wealth would not in fact explode. The book's UK relevance is total: it underpins Resolution Foundation's diagnosis that **UK household wealth rose from 4.1× GDP in 1997 to 6.3× GDP in 2024**, and frames every Wealth Tax Commission argument. For your purposes it is indispensable because it gives you the language for inheritance, patrimonial wealth, and why a one-off wealth tax can be conceptually different from an annual one.

***Capital and Ideology* (2020)** is more programmatic. Piketty argues inequality regimes are political-ideological choices and proposes "participatory socialism" — top tax rates up to 90%, a €120,000 capital endowment at 25, worker codetermination. Milanovic calls the historical sweep impressive but the policy programme politically unmoored. It is *not* required reading if time-constrained — *A Brief History of Equality* (2022) is a 300-page distillation suitable for skim.

**Anthony Atkinson, *Inequality: What Can Be Done?* (2015)** is the most UK-specific work in the canon and the best bridge from diagnosis to programme. Atkinson — who with Piketty created the modern top-incomes literature — offers 15 concrete proposals: a 65% top income-tax rate, guaranteed public-sector jobs at minimum wage, a universal capital endowment at adulthood, a progressive lifetime capital-receipts tax. His central claim is that less inequality requires redesigning technology, employment, social security, capital ownership, and taxation *together*, not just "taxing the rich a bit more". The main criticism to keep in view is practical rather than theoretical: Atkinson is more convincing on what an egalitarian policy stack looks like than on what coalitions, administrative capacity, and political timing make it passable. The IFS in its Green Budget analyses argues several proposals risk behavioural responses; nonetheless this is the **single most useful book** for translating diagnosis into UK policy.

**Branko Milanovic** matters in three voices. ***Global Inequality* (2016)** introduced the famous "elephant curve" of global income growth 1988–2008 — the global middle class and global top 1% gained most, while the lower-middle of rich countries stagnated. The elephant frames "left-behind" Brexit voting and prevents you from becoming domestically parochial. ***Capitalism, Alone* (2019)** distinguishes "liberal meritocratic" (US) from "political" (China) capitalism, both producing extreme inequality through **homoploutia** (the same individuals earning top labour *and* top capital incomes), assortative mating, and inheritance. ***Visions of Inequality* (2023)** is the intellectual history of how inequality fell out of economics during the Cold War and is now being re-centred. Read the first two; the third can wait.

**Richard Wilkinson and Kate Pickett, *The Spirit Level* (2009)** argues more equal rich countries do better across eleven health and social outcomes via a "status anxiety" mechanism. Statistically contested — Snowdon (2010) and Saunders (2010) argue cherry-picked samples, only one of eleven correlations surviving rigorous scrutiny — but **politically enormous in the UK**, founding the Equality Trust and informing the Marmot Reviews. Read it less as final causal proof and more as a framing text for why inequality is socially corrosive even above the poverty line; later reassessments do not simply dismiss the thesis but conclude inequality matters in interaction with broader institutional variables such as democratic quality and social organisation. *The Inner Level* (2018) extends the psychological argument; skim only.

**Gabriel Zucman, *The Hidden Wealth of Nations* (2015)** documents that approximately 8% of household financial wealth — over $7 trillion at time of writing — sits in tax havens. The core point is that wealth inequality is not just about who owns what, but about what states can actually **see, measure, and tax**; Zucman's offshore work is also crucial for understanding why national balance sheets and top-end wealth can be mismeasured. The UK and its Crown Dependencies (BVI, Cayman, Bermuda, Jersey) are the single largest enabler of corporate tax abuse globally according to Tax Justice Network. For Britain this is central: the UK's role in global finance, debates over trusts and beneficial ownership, and the post-2025 non-dom reforms all make much more sense once you see how secrecy infrastructures shape both inequality and policy capacity. Pair this with Zucman's 2024 G20 report on a coordinated minimum tax on ultra-high-net-worth individuals.

**Brett Christophers, *Rentier Capitalism* (2020)** is the best UK-facing bridge from abstract inequality to sectoral structure. Christophers maps the UK economy across seven extractive sectors (financial, natural-resource, IP, platform, infrastructure, outsourcing, contract-and-land rents). The criticism to remember is that "rent" can become too broad a category if used loosely; the concept is strongest when tied to concrete sectors and institutional mechanisms. For the UK, that is exactly what makes the book valuable: it pushes you toward housing, utilities, land, and asset ownership rather than treating inequality as just a tax-schedule problem. Christophers is the most important UK-applied political economist working today and you will return to his other books in Tier 2.

If you want a seventh foundational text, make it either **Walter Scheidel's *The Great Leveler* (2017)** — the most pessimistic book in the canon, arguing only mass-mobilisation war, transformative revolution, state collapse, and lethal pandemics have meaningfully compressed inequality (critics including Bengtsson cite Finland and Norway 1850–1914 as counterexamples of peaceful levelling; Resolution Foundation's *Inequality Control* 2024 shows COVID did *not* compress UK wealth) — or **Mariana Mazzucato's *The Value of Everything* (2018)**, which is the cleanest conceptual antidote to lazy talk about "wealth creators" because it asks when profit reflects value creation and when it reflects value extraction. That distinction runs through UK arguments about finance, private equity, privatisation, land, and tech. Read Scheidel to discipline optimism; read Mazzucato to sharpen the value/extraction distinction.

Finally, **Joseph Stiglitz's *The Price of Inequality* (2012)** and **Angus Deaton's *The Great Escape* (2013)** are useful supplements. Stiglitz frames top-end concentration as rent-seeking rather than marginal productivity — the analytical scaffolding for Christophers' UK rentier-capitalism work. Deaton matters mostly because he chairs the **IFS Deaton Review of Inequalities** (2019–2026), the most comprehensive UK inequality review of the 2020s. Read Deaton primarily for context.

| **Piketty & Saez, "Income Inequality in the United States 1913–1998"** (*QJE* 2003) | Top 1% US income share fell from ~18% (1913) to ~8% (late 1970s), then rose to ~17% (1998). | Replaced top-coded surveys with administrative tax data, exposing massive understatement of top concentration. | **Auten & Splinter, "Income Inequality in the United States"** (*JPE* 2024) | Top 1% after-tax national-income share rose by ~1pp only, not 5–10pp as PSZ find. | The current most consequential methodological dispute in the field. Read alongside Piketty–Saez–Zucman 2018. |

(Twelve, not ten — but the field has moved on since "the ten papers" became conventional.)

### The UK-specific essential reports

Five UK-focused documents are essential supplements to the international canon.

The **IFS Deaton Review of Inequalities** (2019–2026), chaired by Angus Deaton with Robert Joyce, James Banks, Richard Blundell, Paul Johnson and others, has produced 79 papers across income, wealth, health, education, geography, gender, ethnicity and political power. The final book, *Challenging Inequalities*, appears in 2026.

The **Mirrlees Review** (IFS, 2010–11) — *Dimensions of Tax Design* and *Tax by Design* — remains the most comprehensive UK tax-reform proposal in three decades, recommending alignment of capital and labour income tax rates.

**Resolution Foundation's annual wealth audits** (most recently *Before the Fall*, October 2025) provide the year-on-year UK distribution picture; total household wealth £17tn in 2020–22, of which 48% is pensions and 32% property.

**Advani, Hughson & Tarrant's revenue and distributional modelling** for the Wealth Tax Commission (*Fiscal Studies* 2021) is the operational UK proposal.

**Advani, Hughson & Summers' "How much tax do the rich really pay?"** (*Oxford Review of Economic Policy* 2023) shows the effective average tax rate on UK individuals *falls* above ~£500k due to capital-gains preferences — the empirical heart of the UK fairness argument.

## Tier 2 — The broader picture

Once the canon is internalised, five sub-fields supply the texture. Approach this tier opportunistically rather than completely: read one or two books from each, skim the rest.

### Political economy of capitalism

**Ha-Joon Chang's *23 Things They Don't Tell You About Capitalism* (2010)** and ***Bad Samaritans* (2007)** are the accessible-polemical entry to heterodox developmental economics. Tim Harford and Diane Coyle have argued Chang caricatures mainstream economics; nonetheless he is widely cited by UK Labour figures and his SOAS-school framing of industrial policy underwrites Mazzucato's later work.

**Acemoglu and Robinson's *Why Nations Fail* (2012)** and ***The Narrow Corridor* (2019)** propose "inclusive vs extractive" institutions and a "narrow corridor" between weak and despotic states. Milanovic and others argue the inclusive/extractive binary is tautological.

**Acemoglu and Johnson's *Power and Progress* (2023)** — boosted by Acemoglu's 2024 Nobel — argues technology does not automatically deliver shared prosperity; bargaining power and democratic institutions determine distribution. Most relevant in the UK for current AI-policy debates around the Bletchley summit and the Labour government's industrial strategy.

**Mariana Mazzucato's *The Entrepreneurial State* (2013)** reframes the state as the under-acknowledged "investor of first resort" behind major innovations (touchscreens, GPS, mRNA), arguing public risk-bearing should be matched with public returns. The book heavily influences UK industrial-strategy thinking at UCL IIPP and the case for a UK National Wealth Fund. ***The Value of Everything* (2018)** distinguishes value-creation from value-extraction; ***Mission Economy* (2021)** proposes Apollo-style mission-oriented public direction-setting and informs Starmer's "five missions." Critics (Stian Westlake, Alberto Mingardi) argue she conflates funding with entrepreneurship.

**Brett Christophers** is the most important UK-applied political economist working today. Beyond *Rentier Capitalism*, ***The New Enclosure* (2018)** documents that approximately **half of UK publicly-owned land has been privatised since 1979** — the largest land transfer since the Norman Conquest. ***Our Lives in Their Portfolios* (2023)** examines asset-manager society — Blackstone, BlackRock, Brookfield, Macquarie — and is essential UK reading. ***The Price is Wrong* (2024)** explains why cheap renewables aren't profitable enough to drive a fast transition under current electricity-market design, with explicit UK relevance to GB Energy and the failed AR5 offshore-wind auction (2023).

**David Graeber's *Debt: The First 5000 Years* (2011)** is the most theoretically ambitious volume in this section — the "myth of barter" debunked, debt as moral-political relationship, periodic jubilees as historical norm. Chris Hann and economic historians dispute several specifics. ***Bullshit Jobs* (2018)** is essentially polemical; useful for the productivity-puzzle and four-day-week debates but methodologically thin. Read *Debt*; skim *Bullshit Jobs*. (Graeber is an anthropological counterpoint to economics, which is its value — but for that reason he belongs in this tier rather than in the canon.)

### UK housing and land — the central battlefield

Housing is where UK wealth inequality becomes most visible and most politically inflammable, and where the literature is unusually rich. Start with **Josh Ryan-Collins's *Why Can't You Afford a Home?* (2018)** for the foundational argument: the housing crisis is fundamentally a credit-and-land problem, not a building problem. His **housing-finance feedback cycle** — deregulated mortgage credit chasing inelastic land supply — produces price spirals regardless of construction levels. With Lloyd and Macfarlane he extended this in *Rethinking the Economics of Land and Housing* (2017), the defining text for the UK land-economy revival.

**Danny Dorling's *Shattered Nation* (2023)** is the flagship synthesis of UK post-austerity inequality scholarship — Britain as the most unequal large European economy, with living standards peaking in 1974. Reviewers (LSE Review of Books, Literary Review) note the book is heavier on diagnosis than transformative agenda; nonetheless it is the most widely-reviewed UK inequality book of recent years. ***All That Is Solid* (2014)** challenges the supply-side housing consensus on geographer grounds; ***Inequality and the 1%* (2014/2019)** is the popular UK companion to Piketty.

**The supply-versus-demand debate** is the central live policy dispute. **Ian Mulheirn** (now Tony Blair Institute) argues English housing supply has outstripped household formation since the 1990s, vacant homes have risen, and price inflation chiefly reflects falling real interest rates not insufficient construction. Critics (Shelter, Resolution Foundation, Jonathan Portes, Paul Cheshire at LSE) argue Mulheirn underweights distributional mismatch and overstates ONS household-projection adequacy. The debate is genuinely unresolved among serious UK economists, and Labour's planning reform politics depends on which side is right. Read Mulheirn's blog posts and the responses; do *not* settle the question prematurely.

The empirical realisation of asset-manager society in UK housing is striking. **Blackstone-backed Leaf Living acquired ~4,500 homes from Vistry in two deals worth £1.4bn in 2023–24** and now manages ~17,000 UK residential properties. Lloyds' Citra Living targets 50,000 rental homes by 2030. Single-family build-to-rent rose from 5% of UK rental investment in 2019 to over 50% by 2024. The Leasehold and Freehold Reform Act 2024 (Royal Assent May 2024) and the draft Commonhold Bill 2026 represent the most significant tenure reform in a generation; implementation has been slow due to drafting flaws and freeholder judicial reviews.

### Tax policy — the operational frontier

Beyond Zucman's *Hidden Wealth of Nations* and his 2024 G20 proposal for a coordinated 2% minimum tax on billionaire wealth (*The Triumph of Injustice*, 2019, with Saez, provides the methodological template for the Advani–Summers UK work), the essential context is **Tax Justice Network's *State of Tax Justice* 2024**, which estimates global losses of **~$492bn/year**, with 43% enabled by the eight countries opposed to a UN tax convention, of which the UK is the most consequential.

**Richard Murphy's *The Joy of Tax* (2015)** and Tax Research UK blog (renamed Funding the Future, 2023) are widely read but heterodox; his **estimated UK tax gap of ~£90bn** (vs HMRC's ~£36bn) is contested. Take Murphy's empirical claims as positions in active debate, not consensus figures.

The UK academic spine on wealth taxation is **Arun Advani (Warwick), Andy Summers (LSE) and Emma Chamberlain (Pump Court)**, who together commissioned the **Wealth Tax Commission** and now run **CenTax** (Centre for the Analysis of Taxation, founded 2024). Their Wealth Tax Commission final report (December 2020) recommended that *if* the UK chose to raise substantial new revenue, a **one-off wealth tax** would be preferable to higher income tax, NICs or VAT — and preferable to an annual wealth tax. Headline estimates: a one-off 5% (1% over 5 years) levy above **£500,000 per individual would raise approximately £260bn** net of 10% non-compliance; the same structure above **£2m raises ~£80bn**. The Commission did *not* recommend an annual wealth tax because administrative costs exceed plausible revenue at low thresholds.

Advani–Burgherr–Summers (CAGE 2022, *EJ* 2025) used HMRC microdata to estimate the **migration elasticity of UK super-rich at just 0.02–0.26** in response to the 2017 deemed-domicile reform — well below the level needed for revenue loss from a wealth tax. This work directly underpinned Hunt's March 2024 partial abolition of non-dom status and Reeves's October 2024 completion of the reform, projected to raise **~£3.2bn/year**.

**Crucial conceptual point**: "tax wealth" is not one proposal. It could mean an annual net-wealth tax, a one-off levy, higher property taxation, capital-gains reform, inheritance-tax redesign, pension-tax reform, non-dom reform, or anti-avoidance enforcement. A lot of shallow UK debate comes from pretending these are interchangeable.

### Financialisation

**Adair Turner's *Between Debt and the Devil* (2015)** is the most rigorous volume here: most credit in modern economies funds existing real estate not productive investment; debt should be taxed as a pollutant; banks need far higher capital. Turner chaired the FSA 2008–13; his **Turner Review** is the UK's foundational post-crisis regulatory document.

**Ann Pettifor's *The Production of Money* (2017)** explains endogenous money creation by commercial banks (95% of broad money), now confirmed by the Bank of England's own 2014 Quarterly Bulletin. Her 2006 *The Coming First World Debt Crisis* predicted 2008.

**Grace Blakeley's *Stolen* (2019)** and ***Vulture Capitalism* (2024)** are the leading young-left UK accounts; *Vulture Capitalism* was longlisted for the Women's Prize.

**Rana Foroohar's *Makers and Takers* (2016)** and ***Homecoming* (2022)** are US-focused but the financialisation framework transfers directly; *Homecoming* in particular is useful for connecting financialisation to supply-chain and locational politics, which matters for UK levelling-up debates.

**Michael Hudson's *Killing the Host* (2015)** introduces the FIRE-sector parasite framing widely cited by Christophers and the rentier-capitalism school; Hudson's heterodox positions and political associations should be flagged.

Read these *after* the housing block. Once you already understand rent, property, and debt, these books stop sounding like broad anti-finance polemics and start reading as institutional diagnoses of how balance sheets, supply chains, and capital markets reallocate bargaining power.

### Technology and inequality

**Shoshana Zuboff's *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019)** is foundational for UK Online Safety Act debates and ICO investigations of adtech; LSE Review critics (Sam DiBella, Evgeny Morozov in NLR) note Zuboff lacks a theory of collective action and treats surveillance capitalism as a "rogue" mutation rather than capitalist continuation.

**Cathy O'Neil's *Weapons of Math Destruction* (2016)** is the readable entry to algorithmic inequality, directly relevant to the 2020 A-level algorithm scandal and DWP fraud-detection algorithms.

**Safiya Noble's *Algorithms of Oppression* (2018)** focuses on racial and gender bias in search.

**Daniel Susskind's *A World Without Work* (2020)** and **Aaron Benanav's *Automation and the Future of Work* (2020)** sit at opposite poles of the automation debate. Susskind, who served on UK government strategy and AI advisory panels, argues advances in machine learning are gathering decisive substituting force; Benanav counters that persistent labour under-demand reflects secular stagnation, not runaway automation. Read both as a pair.

**Wendy Liu's *Abolish Silicon Valley* (2020)**, published by London-based Repeater Books, is part-memoir part-manifesto and influential in UK left-tech circles (Common Knowledge, Autonomy). Together with Zuboff and Noble, it moves you from platform power and behavioural extraction, to biased information infrastructures, to the political economy myths of the tech industry itself. If you want to write or speak about how software and data systems concentrate wealth, this trio is much stronger foundation than generic "AI will replace jobs" commentary.

## Tier 3 — Quantitative foundations

This tier exists to ensure your numerical claims about wealth inequality are *yours*, derived from data, not borrowed from Gary Stevenson or anyone else. Given your CS/maths background and game-theory publication, you can skip the gentle introductions and route directly to the methodological core.

With your background you do **not** need a full undergraduate economics retraining programme. You do need the subset of economics that lets you read inequality research without bluffing: welfare economics, public finance, labour economics, urban/housing economics, macro-finance, and the parts of development economics that deal with mobility and state capacity. On the quantitative side, the credibility threshold is: survey design and weighting, equivalisation, quantiles and top shares, index-number thinking, decomposition, causal inference, and the limits of observational data. **The decisive skill is not formal cleverness; it is being able to say exactly what a statistic measures, what it omits, and what assumptions sit underneath it.**

### The free course backbone

The single most important free resource is **CORE Econ**, particularly **"Economy, Society, and Public Policy" (ESPP)** and **"The Economy 2.0"**. ESPP Section 5.9 is the canonical Lorenz/Gini construction; the companion **"Doing Economics" project** provides Python/R empirical exercises on real datasets. The **CORE Insights "A world of differences: an introduction to inequality"** by Sethi, Naidu and Thomas is a standalone module worth a week of focused study.

For applied micro, **Raj Chetty's Harvard Ec 50 "Using Big Data to Solve Economic and Social Problems"** is the best free graduate-adjacent course. The full lecture videos, slides, problem sets *and raw datasets* are on opportunityinsights.org/course. Chetty himself lectures; he covers equality of opportunity, geographic mobility, education, race, health, crime and climate using regression, IV, RDD and DiD informally. For a CS person it is perfectly pitched.

For labour economics, **MIT 14.661 (Acemoglu and Angrist, available on OCW)** is the single best free graduate course bearing on inequality — Acemoglu lectures on social mobility, peer effects, human capital; Angrist on "MaC-Metrics" (matching/IV). Prerequisites are intermediate micro and econometrics at the level of *Mostly Harmless*. **MIT 14.03/14.003x (Autor)** is application-driven (minimum wage, EITC, insurance markets, signalling) and free to audit on MITx; this is your formal applied-micro grounding.

For econometrics, **Angrist and Pischke's *Mostly Harmless Econometrics* (2009)** and ***Mastering 'Metrics* (2014)** are the standard pairing. Cameron and Trivedi's *Microeconometrics: Methods and Applications* is the advanced reference. **Frank Cowell's *Measuring Inequality* (3rd ed., free at darp.lse.ac.uk/MI3)** is the indispensable methods text — chapters 1–5 give you the axiomatic foundations of Gini, Atkinson, and Generalised Entropy classes.

For statistical refreshers if needed, **OpenIntro Statistics** and **Introduction to Econometrics with R** are both free, rigorous, and accessible.

### Data sources

**WID.world** for pre-tax/post-tax/wealth shares across ~200 countries; **World Bank PIP** for headcount and Gini from harmonised household surveys; **OECD Income Distribution Database** for harmonised OECD members; **LIS/LWS** for gold-standard harmonised microdata (free Key Figures tier, plus DART web tool, plus LISSY remote-execution); **UK Wealth and Assets Survey via UK Data Service** with two critical methodological caveats — first, the **December 2024 ONS methodology change for valuing defined-benefit pensions** cut measured UK total wealth by approximately **£2.3 trillion** (the IFS argues this is economically wrong; both sides should be presented); and second, the **Office for Statistics Regulation suspended WAS's accredited official statistics status in June 2025** following quality concerns, which means any claim you make from WAS data must now be explicitly hedged.

### Methodological debates you need to internalise

Seven controversies sit at the heart of distributional research, and you should be able to discuss each clearly.

The **Gini-versus-Palma debate** matters because the Gini is not subgroup decomposable, is more sensitive to transfers around the mode than to the tails, and can give identical values to very different distributions. The **Palma ratio** (top 10% / bottom 40%) — proposed by Cobham and Sumner (2013) — is policy-relevant, transparent, tail-sensitive and easier for non-specialists; 90 economists including Stiglitz endorsed it for SDG inclusion. The defence of the Gini (Cowell) is that its failures are correctable by reporting the full distribution and complementary indices. Report both.

**PPP versus market exchange rates** for global comparison: PPP conversion inflates poor-country incomes 3–5× relative to MER because non-traded services are cheap in low-income countries. Critics (Hickel, Reddy and Minoiu) argue PPP systematically understates global inequality; Milanovic now presents both. For cross-country poverty thresholds use PPP; for relative purchasing power of rich-anywhere vs poor-anywhere, MER is more meaningful.

**Pre-tax versus post-tax/transfer** measurement: the US Gini for disposable income is ~0.39 versus ~0.50 for market income — taxes and transfers cut measured inequality by ~22%. The DINA framework distinguishes market income, pre-tax national income (including pensions as deferred wages), disposable income, and post-tax post-transfer national income. The choice of concept drives most of the disagreement.

**Income versus wealth versus consumption**: a country can look middling on disposable-income inequality and still be brutally unequal on housing wealth or inheritances. The UK is the exemplar of this divergence — its disposable-income Gini sits around the OECD median while its wealth-to-GDP ratio is one of the world's highest.

**Before versus after housing costs** (UK-specific): UK poverty and inequality numbers change materially once you treat rent and mortgage burden as central rather than incidental. The DWP's *Households Below Average Income* (HBAI) statistics publish both AHC and BHC measures for exactly this reason. Always state which you are using.

The **Auten–Splinter (*JPE* 2024) versus Piketty–Saez–Zucman dispute** is the most consequential current methodological controversy. PSZ estimate the US top 1% pre-tax national-income share rose from ~10% (1980) to ~19% (2019); Auten–Splinter, using the same data with different allocation assumptions, find the share roughly flat at ~9–11% with a much smaller rise. The disagreement reduces to (a) allocation of underreported income — PSZ assume it scales with reported income (concentrated), Auten–Splinter use IRS audit studies (more diffuse), (b) allocation of untaxed capital income and corporate retained earnings, (c) allocation of government consumption and deficits, (d) dependent-filer adjustments. The WIL/PSZ 2024 rebuttal argues the dispute reduces to assumptions about specific allocations; Auerbach–Burman–Reck–Iselin (NBER WP 33678, 2025) returns to Haig–Simons first principles. **For UK use: report both bounds; do not headline a single number without methodology.**

The **Smith–Zidar–Zwick (*QJE* 2023) critique** of Saez–Zucman wealth concentration shows that rates of return are systematically higher at the top *within* each asset class — the rich earn ~3.5× the average return on fixed income. Using heterogeneous capitalisation factors they find a top-1% wealth share of 31.5% (2016) — large, but smaller than Saez–Zucman's ~40%. Implications for wealth-tax revenue estimates and for the "r > g" mechanism are substantial.

Finally, the **survey–tax–national-accounts trilemma**: surveys (FRS, EU-SILC, HFCS, SCF, WAS) under-sample the rich and top-code; tax records capture the upper tail but only fiscal income; national accounts give macro-consistent totals but no distribution. DINA reconciles all three, but allocation assumptions drive the disagreements. For UK wealth specifically, the **WAS has no oversampling of the top tail** (unlike SCF), the SCAPE-vs-gilts pension valuation debate is unresolved, and (as noted) WAS lost accredited status in June 2025.

## Tier 4 — The UK policy landscape

This is where your six months should pay off most directly: the ability to discuss UK wealth-tax proposals with operational specificity.

### The live UK proposals

The **Wealth Tax Commission (December 2020)** remains the central reference. Its worked examples are still the reference points for public debate: a one-off tax charged at 1% a year for five years above £500,000 per individual could raise around **£260 billion**, while the same design above £2 million could raise around **£80 billion**; by contrast, an *annual* tax above £2 million at about 0.6% is presented as an example that could raise around £10 billion a year, but with higher avoidance, valuation, and liquidity problems.

**Patriotic Millionaires UK** proposes a **2% annual wealth tax on net assets above £10m**, claimed to raise **~£24bn/year** affecting ~20,000 individuals (~0.04% of the UK population). Key spokespeople include Julia Davies, Dale Vince, Stephen Kinsella and Phil White; Gary Stevenson is a high-profile public endorser.

**Tax Justice UK and Patriotic Millionaires' joint 2025 package** bundles ten reforms claimed to raise £60bn+: the 2% wealth tax (£24bn), equalising CGT with income tax (up to £14bn), applying NI to investment income (£10.2bn), closing IHT loopholes (£1.4bn), closing non-dom replacement loopholes (£1bn), 4% on share buybacks (£2bn), scrapping the NI Upper Earnings Limit cap.

**IPPR's Commission on Economic Justice** *Prosperity and Justice* (2018) provided 73 recommendations including the principle that wealth income should be taxed at labour rates; IPPR's *A Wealth of Difference* (Roberts, Blakeley, Murphy 2018) quantified £90bn over five years from CGT equalisation. IPPR has also argued that the current taxation of wealth worsens regional divides and proposed using wealth-tax reform as part of regional rebalancing.

**Resolution Foundation's** wealth programme (Brewer, Corlett, Broome, Pittaway, Leslie) produces the most operationally credible UK distribution work. RF does *not* favour a new net-wealth tax; it prefers deep reform of capital gains, inheritance, pensions, and especially property taxation.

The current view from the **Institute for Fiscal Studies**, sharpened by the 2026 Deaton Review outputs, is similar to RF's: if the goal is better taxation of wealth, fixing existing capital taxes is usually easier and cleaner than launching an annual wealth tax from scratch.

**NEF** advocates structural visibility — asset registries, an HMRC HNW Individuals unit, business-rates reform replacing rates with a two-rate land/property tax.

### The history of UK wealth-tax attempts

The **1974 Labour wealth tax proposal** — Healey's manifesto pledge — was abandoned by 1976. Glennerster's 2012 *Journal of Social Policy* paper, using declassified Treasury files, concluded civil-service opposition was decisive: officials calculated administrative and valuation costs exceeded likely revenue.

The **Poll Tax / Community Charge (1989–93)** was a flat per-adult tax replacing domestic rates — Scotland April 1989, England/Wales April 1990. The 31 March 1990 Trafalgar Square riot drew 200,000 protesters; £1.2bn went uncollected in year one. The tax precipitated Thatcher's November 1990 downfall.

**Council Tax (April 1993)** uses banded property valuation still based on **1 April 1991 values that have never been revalued in England**.

The **Bedroom Tax (under-occupancy penalty, April 2013)** cut housing benefit 14% for one spare bedroom, 25% for two-plus, affecting ~660,000 households (two-thirds with a disabled member). LSE/Gibbons–Sánchez-Vidal (2018) found ~70% of expected savings achieved without material mobility increase.

**Non-dom status**, introduced by Pitt the Younger in 1799 with domicile as qualifier from the Finance Act 1914, was reformed in 2008 (£30k RBC), 2017 (deemed dom at 15/20 years), and abolished by Hunt (March 2024) and Reeves (October 2024) effective April 2025. The old remittance basis was replaced by a four-year foreign-income-and-gains regime for qualifying arrivals after ten years of non-UK residence, alongside wider changes to trusts and transitional rules. The Advani–Burgherr–Summers work was decisive in overcoming Treasury "no revenue" arguments. **OBR projection: ~£2.7–3bn/year** from abolition.

**Capital Gains Tax** has cycled through indexation (1982), Lawson income-tax-alignment (1988), taper relief (1998), Entrepreneurs' Relief (2008), and Reeves's 30 October 2024 increases to **18%/24% main rates**.

**Inheritance Tax** retains the nil-rate band at £325,000 frozen since 2009 (now to 2030); only ~3.7% of estates pay; receipts ~£7bn. Reeves's October 2024 budget restricted BPR/APR to 100% relief on the first £1m and 50% thereafter from April 2026, provoking the November 2024 farmer protests. From April 2027, unused pension funds fall within the IHT estate.

**Pensions Lifetime Allowance abolition** (Hunt, March 2023, effective April 2024) cost ~£800m/year, stated rationale being NHS clinician retention, widely criticised as a windfall for the wealthy. Labour pledged reversal but did not deliver.

Each of these episodes matters because they show a different failure mode: head taxes can be seen as flatly unfair; housing-related cuts can be politically toxic when they hit people with little room to adjust; elite-focused reforms produce complexity and transitional carve-outs; and capital-tax reform is easier to sell when it looks like base-cleaning rather than confiscation.

### The strongest counterarguments — steelmanned

You should be able to state the case *against* wealth taxation more clearly than its opponents.

**Capital flight** is the headline concern: Norway's 2022 wealth-tax hike coincided with ~80+ wealthy emigrants in 2022–23, mostly to Switzerland; by 2024 ~300 millionaires had left. However Norwegian wealth-tax revenues actually rose from 27bn kr (2022) to estimates of 34bn kr (2025). UK migration elasticity from the 2017 non-dom reform was 0.02–0.26 — well below the level needed for revenue loss. Young–Varner US state-level studies find elasticities around 0.1%.

**Valuation problems** for illiquid assets (private business, art, agricultural land, DB pensions) are real; the 1970s UK Green Paper foundered partly on this.

**Administrative costs** historically ran 25–50% of revenue raised in European wealth taxes (OECD 2018).

**Behavioural responses** are large in Switzerland — Brülhart, Gruber, Krapf, Schmidheiny (*AEJ:EP* 2022) find a 1pp drop in cantonal wealth tax raises reported wealth by ~43% over six years — but Jakobsen, Jakobsen, Kleven and Zucman's Danish work shows that with **third-party reporting** responses are far smaller.

**The European abolitions** matter: Austria (1994), Denmark (1996), Germany (suspended 1997 after the Bundesverfassungsgericht ruled in 1995 that unequal real-estate-vs-financial-asset valuation violated constitutional equality), Netherlands (2001, replaced with deemed-return Box 3 itself ruled unconstitutional 2021–22), Finland (2006), Sweden (2007), France (ISF replaced with real-estate-only IFI in 2018). By 2017 only four OECD countries levied recurrent net wealth taxes; the OECD's 2018 *Role and Design of Net Wealth Taxes* concluded reforming existing capital taxes is generally preferable to introducing a new annual wealth tax.

**The "double taxation" argument** — that wealth has already been taxed as income — is rhetorically powerful but analytically weak once you note (a) much top wealth is unrealised capital gains never taxed, (b) inheritances were not earned by recipients, and (c) the tax treatment of carried interest, BPR/APR and pension lump sums creates substantial untaxed top-end accumulation.

### How proponents respond

Three responses matter most.

**Switzerland** demonstrates that an annual wealth tax can work as ~3.6% of total tax revenue with rates 0.1–1%, broad base and constitutional entrenchment — though large cantonal mobility responses show design matters.

**Norway** retains a wealth tax with revenues continuing to grow despite emigration.

**Spain's Solidarity Tax (Impuesto Temporal de Solidaridad de las Grandes Fortunas)**, introduced December 2022, sits above €3m at 1.7–3.5% as a national floor neutralising regional rebates. **Among Spanish fortunes above €30m, wealth-tax compliance jumped from 27.5% (2022) to 99% (2023); revenue rose 60% to €1.97bn.** This is the best modern operational evidence that well-designed wealth taxation works.

Saez–Zucman's anti-avoidance toolkit (*Triumph of Injustice* 2019) includes comprehensive base, aggressive third-party reporting, 40% exit tax on unrealised gains, whistleblower bounties, and international information-exchange. Advani–Summers' Wealth Tax Commission evidence papers explicitly address every standard objection — administrative costs via Burgherr's Evidence Paper, liquidity via instalments and pension-lump-sum deferral, behavioural responses via Advani–Tarrant, trust handling via Chamberlain. Third-party reporting is the critical anti-avoidance mechanism.

Proponents also increasingly concede part of the critic's case and shift toward a more practical agenda: **reform capital gains, inheritance, pensions, and land/property taxes first, and reserve a wealth tax for exceptional circumstances or explicitly redistributionist goals.** That is where the UK expert centre of gravity sits today.

## Tier 5 — History and context

UK inequality became what it is through a sequence of identifiable political choices over 80 years. The compressed Attlee-to-Wilson distribution — Gini around 0.25–0.26 by the late 1970s, top 1% income share fallen from over 16% pre-war to ~6% by 1979 — depended on top marginal income-tax rates of 90%+ (peak 98%), strong unions (peak ~13 million members), full employment, the council-house programme, and the welfare-state expansion built on Beveridge.

That settlement broke through three stages.

First, the **breakdown of Bretton Woods (1971–73)**, the 1973 oil shock (UK inflation 24% by 1975), and the **1976 IMF bailout** marked the political defeat of post-war Keynesianism.

Second, **Thatcher (1979–90)** cut the top income-tax rate from 83% to 60% (1979) then to 40% (Lawson budget 1988); introduced **Right to Buy (Housing Act 1980)** under which roughly 2 million council homes sold by the mid-1990s; deregulated the City of London via the **Big Bang (27 October 1986)**; defeated the NUM in the 1984–85 miners' strike; passed sequential Employment and Trade Union Acts restricting secondary picketing and closed shops; and privatised BT (1984), British Gas (1986), BA (1987), British Steel (1988), water (1989), electricity (1990–91), and (under Major) British Rail (1994–97). UK Gini for disposable income rose from ~0.24 (1979) to ~0.34 (1991) — the fastest rise of any OECD country.

Third, **New Labour (1997–2010)** under Mandelson's "intensely relaxed about people getting filthy rich" doctrine pursued light-touch financial regulation while introducing redistribution via tax credits, the **national minimum wage (April 1999)**, Sure Start and NHS investment. Net effect: inequality stayed broadly stable but the top 1% pulled away; the disposable-income Gini reached ~0.36 pre-2008. **Bank of England independence (1997)** removed monetary policy from political control.

**Austerity from 2010** launched ~£30bn in welfare cuts and 20% real departmental cuts. **Real wages remained below 2008 levels for ~15 years — the longest stagnation since the Napoleonic era.**

**Brexit (referendum 23 June 2016, exit 31 January 2020)** has reduced trade volumes ~15% below counterfactual and long-run GDP ~4% below counterfactual per OBR/Bank estimates.

**COVID (2020–22)** drove a K-shaped recovery; UK wealth rose £900bn through 2020 with gains heavily skewed to asset-owners.

**The cost-of-living crisis (2022–)** saw the energy price cap surge to a peak ~£4,279 equivalent in early 2023, CPI inflation peak at 11.1% (October 2022), and mortgage rates spike following Truss–Kwarteng's September 2022 mini-budget (£45bn unfunded tax cuts crashing sterling and gilts).

The UK now stands at OECD disposable-income Gini ~0.355 (ONS reports 32.9% for FYE 2024) versus Germany ~0.30, France ~0.30, Netherlands ~0.29, Nordics ~0.25–0.28 and the US ~0.395 — consistently the most unequal large Western European economy on disposable income. UK wealth Gini is ~0.62, lower than US ~0.85 but rising in real terms as wealth-to-GDP climbed from ~280% (1980) to ~710% (2019).

The Deaton Review data make the intergenerational dimension impossible to ignore: cohorts born in the 1970s and 1980s have fallen far behind their predecessors on home ownership, while older cohorts benefited from low rates and large capital gains. Separate IFS work shows inheritances are set to matter more for younger generations as older cohorts hold more wealth and younger cohorts do not enjoy stronger income growth. **British inequality is increasingly about wealth transmission, not just annual wages.**

### International lessons for the UK

The Nordic point is the most counterintuitive: **most Nordics abolished their wealth taxes** (Denmark 1996, Finland 2006, Sweden 2007), retaining only Norway's. Nordic equality is delivered chiefly through high progressive income tax (top rates 55–60%), universal high-quality public services, collective bargaining coverage of 70–90% of the workforce (versus ~26% in the UK), universal childcare and wage compression via central agreements. **For UK debate this matters: equalising outcomes via wages, services and unions is more powerful than tax-and-transfer alone**, even if a wealth tax is also worth pursuing.

The French ISF-to-IFI reform (2018) shows the political fragility of recurrent wealth taxes when the base is too narrow; the **Zucman tax** debate (a 2% floor on wealth >€100m) is the live successor proposal. The Swiss case shows cantonal taxes work but produce large within-country mobility. The Spanish solidarity tax — €3m threshold, 1.7–3.5%, raising compliance from 27.5% to 99% among €30m+ fortunes — is the most directly applicable recent design experiment for UK purposes.

The US case shows what happens when none of this is done. Reaganomics cut top rates from 70% to 28% (1981–88); Bush tax cuts (2001, 2003) and TCJA 2017 entrenched the regime. **The Saez–Zucman finding that US top 400 households now pay a lower average effective tax rate than the bottom 50% defines the ceiling of where extreme inequality can run** absent counter-mobilisation. The UK is not the US — weaker unions and weaker safety nets in the US, plus much higher wealth inequality (~32% top 1% wealth share versus ~22% UK) — but the trajectory is the warning.

## The special angle — your game-theory edge

Most inequality researchers come from economics or sociology backgrounds and treat strategic interaction either as a black box ("behavioural response") or as a one-shot decision. **Your game-theory and multi-agent cooperation background gives you an unusual angle** that almost nobody in the UK inequality discourse currently occupies. You should develop three signatures deliberately: a **mechanism-design signature** (what can a tax authority know, value, verify, and enforce?), an **agent-based signature** (how do debt, asset-price expectations, inherited wealth, and landlord leverage compound over time?), and a **game-theoretic signature** (how do elites coordinate around exemptions, narrative framing, capital mobility, and policy sequencing?). Below are the productive intersections, ordered by how publishable and politically live they currently are.

### Mechanism design for wealth-tax valuation

The single most administratively difficult issue in UK wealth-tax design — the central reason the 1970s proposal foundered and the central concern in OECD 2018 — is **valuation of illiquid assets** (private business equity, art, agricultural land, DB pensions). Posner and Weyl's **Common Ownership Self-Assessed Tax (COST)** in *Radical Markets* (2018) solves this exactly: owners declare a value; pay annual tax (~7%) on declared value; anyone can compel sale at that price. Truth-telling becomes a dominant strategy in the auction-theoretic limit. The mechanism is mathematically related to Harberger's 1960s Latin American property valuation work. Critics (Matt Clancy, Tyler Cowen) raise dynamic-investment and relationship-specific-investment objections.

**Your potential original contribution**: a *Fiscal Studies* or *Journal of Public Economics* paper titled something like "Self-Assessed Wealth Taxation: A COST-based Mechanism for the UK" — formal mechanism-design analysis of dominant-strategy properties under realistic illiquidity, plus an agent-based simulation of compliance under heterogeneous valuation uncertainty, calibrated to UK Wealth and Assets Survey microdata. This bridges Posner–Weyl with Advani–Chamberlain–Summers; it is publishable, original, and addresses the single biggest UK wealth-tax design objection. Nothing comparable exists in the UK literature.

### Wealth-condensation dynamics — the yard-sale model angle

Bruce Boghosian's series of papers (*IJMPC* 2014, *Physical Review E* 2014, *J Stat Phys* 2015, *Physica A* 2017) developed the **yard-sale model** and its Fokker–Planck description: under fair exchange of fractions of poorer agents' wealth, total wealth condenses on a single agent; adding a wealth tax produces a Pareto-tail steady state via transcritical bifurcation; **wealth-attained advantage** (richer agent wins more often) reproduces oligarchy as a phase transition. Recent extensions on Erdős–Rényi networks (arXiv:2505.04032, 2025) show local but not global condensation; Cardoso, Gonçalves and Iglesias (*Chaos, Solitons & Fractals* 2023) generalise the wealth condensation paradox.

**Your potential contribution**: take Boghosian's framework, calibrate to ONS Wealth and Assets Survey data, embed in a homophily-driven contact network (Jackson-style), make wealth-attained-advantage *endogenous* via network proximity to financial advisers, and use the Wealth Tax Commission's proposed tax structure as an intervention parameter. **The publishable question**: "What is the minimum wealth-tax rate, at what threshold, that prevents oligarchic phase transition in a UK-calibrated yard-sale model?" This publishes simultaneously in *Physica A* / *Journal of Statistical Physics* (your existing tribe) and in policy outlets, and gives Gary Stevenson and the Patriotic Millionaires UK an evidence base they currently lack. This is probably the strongest fit for your existing publication record.

### Coalition formation and the political economy of wealth tax

The Meltzer–Richard (1981) median-voter prediction is that more unequal societies vote for more redistribution — empirically often falsified. Acemoglu and Robinson's *Economic Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy* (2006) provides elite/non-elite bargaining game theory; Ben Ansell's WEALTHPOL project at Nuffield extends this directly to UK housing-wealth politics, with his 2025 paper "Six provocations on the origins and impacts of the UK housing emergency" as the launching point.

**Your potential contribution**: a Shapley/Banzhaf power-index analysis of UK Commons voting coalitions on wealth-tax legislation, integrating homeowner-renter divides with age cohort divides. Ansell's homeowner-wealth politics provides the empirical calibration. The question: "Under what cohort-and-tenure configurations does a wealth tax above £10m clear the Commons?" Coalition-game-theoretic feasibility analysis is your home turf and would be highly novel in UK political economy.

### Tax compliance as a multi-agent network game

Allingham and Sandmo (1972) modelled evasion as a Bernoulli decision; Kleven, Knudsen, Kreiner, Pedersen and Saez (*Econometrica* 2011) showed Danish field-experimental results consistent with the basic model. Alstadsæter, Johannesen and Zucman (*AER* 2019) showed the top 0.01% evade ~25% of taxes versus <5% for others, with ~50% of leaked offshore wealth at the top 0.01%. **Advani, Burgherr and Summers (2025)** estimated a UK super-rich migration elasticity of ~0.26 using HMRC microdata.

**Your potential contribution**: model UK evasion as a coalition problem where wealthy taxpayers and tax advisers form trust networks that lower per-unit evasion costs. Calibrate to the 2017 non-dom deemed-domicile reform and the 2025 non-dom abolition; ask "what audit-targeting strategies achieve dominant-strategy truth-telling from UK non-doms, given that advisers themselves play a cooperation game?" This sits at the intersection of your cooperation work and Advani–Summers–Zucman empirical practice.

### Networks, homophily, and intergenerational mobility

Matthew Jackson's *The Human Network* (2019) and his 2025 paper "Inequality's Economic and Social Roots" (arXiv:2506.13016, forthcoming in the *Econometric Society World Congress* proceedings) identify **homophily in friendship networks → asymmetric job/opportunity information → persistent across-group inequality** even with identical underlying talent. Jackson advocates "policy cocktails" because pure economic interventions cannot fix homophily-driven gaps.

**Your potential contribution**: build a network model of UK labour-market opportunity using accessible LinkedIn/UCAS/HESA microdata where applicants from low-income LSOAs have measurably lower-bandwidth professional networks; quantify the "network multiplier" on the SES graduate earnings premium. This complements Chetty's geographic mobility work (USA) with UK-specific network-mediated mobility analysis.

### The Schelling segregation extension

Schelling (1971) generalises naturally to wealth segregation; Geoff Boeing (2018) and the INET Oxford "Residential income segregation" ABM extend it. Apply to UK LSOA-level income segregation, calibrated using the English Indices of Multiple Deprivation. Add **coalition formation over local-authority planning rules** — residents form coalitions to block development whose payoff depends on house-price externalities. Ties directly to Ansell's housing-wealth politics work and the current Labour planning reform fight.

### Evolutionary game theory and the micro-foundations of redistribution

Sam Bowles' *Microeconomics: Behavior, Institutions, and Evolution* (2004) and Bowles–Gintis's *A Cooperative Species* (2011) argue redistribution and cooperation/inequality regimes are evolutionarily stable equilibria of multi-generational coordination games. **Cultural group selection sustains norms (strong reciprocity, costly punishment) that rational-choice cannot explain.** Bowles and Gintis lead the CORE Econ project; their Santa Fe Institute "Behavioural Sciences" programme continues working on inequality emergence.

**Your potential contribution**: given you publish in cooperation/game theory, you could write a bridging piece arguing that **the most plausible micro-foundation for the Mirrleesian social-welfare function is an evolutionary cooperative-equilibrium argument (à la Bowles), not utilitarianism**. This addresses a real lacuna in optimal-tax theory and could land in *Economics and Philosophy* or *Journal of Economic Methodology*.

### INET Oxford and complexity economics — your most natural institutional home

Doyne Farmer's *Making Sense of Chaos* (Penguin/Yale 2024) is the book-length manifesto for complexity economics. INET Oxford's recent calibrated agent-based work — Wiese et al. (2024) forecasting OECD macro dynamics; Bavaro, Boscolo and Tedeschi (2024) on long-run wealth distribution and intergenerational transfers; the "Sprint to Seventh Carbon Budget" project (2025–26) commissioned by UK DESNZ — represents a sustained UK-relevant computational research programme. Farmer holds the Baillie Gifford Professorship at Oxford; INET Oxford actively recruits and collaborates with multi-agent modellers. **For your career path, INET Oxford may be the single most natural institutional home in the UK.**

### Phase 1 — Foundations (weeks 1–6)

The aim is to internalise the empirical canon and the UK distribution picture quickly, so you can have credible conversations from week 6 onward.

**Week 1 — Piketty's empirical case.** Read *A Brief History of Equality* (2022) cover-to-cover (~6 hours); skim *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* Introduction, Part 1 (chapters 1–2), Part 3 (chapters 7–9 on inequality of labour and capital income), and Part 4 (chapters 13–15 on regulating capital). Begin CORE Econ ESPP units 1–4 in parallel; build a one-page glossary of measurement terms (market income, disposable income, equivalisation, top shares, national accounts, wealth stock, AHC/BHC). Supplementary: listen to the Talking Politics Piketty episodes from the archive. **Milestone**: write a 500-word summary of the r > g argument and the Acemoglu–Robinson 2015 *JEP* critique.

**Week 2 — The UK distribution picture today.** Read Resolution Foundation's *Wealth Check* (July 2024), *Inequality Control* (November 2024), and *Before the Fall* (October 2025). Read the IFS *Living Standards, Poverty and Inequality in the UK 2024* report (Cribb, Joyce, Waters). Skim the Deaton Review summary chapters. Read the latest ONS income bulletin and the OSR decision on WAS accreditation. **Milestone**: produce a one-page UK inequality fact-sheet — wealth-to-GDP ratio, top 10% wealth share, Gini for disposable income (note the 32.9% FYE 2024 ONS figure), share of wealth in pensions, share in property, the median per-adult wealth gap by age cohort.

**Week 3 — Atkinson's policy translation.** Read *Inequality: What Can Be Done?* (2015) cover-to-cover. Skim Atkinson's 1970 *JET* paper "On the measurement of inequality" (it is 20 pages). Begin CORE Econ ESPP Section 5 (Inequality) and the CORE Insights "A world of differences" module. **Milestone**: list Atkinson's 15 proposals and assess each against the current UK policy environment in three sentences; build a "proposal matrix" with columns for mechanism, likely incidence, administrative challenge, and UK political obstacle.

**Week 4 — Milanovic and the global frame.** Read *Global Inequality* (2016) cover-to-cover and *Capitalism, Alone* (2019) selectively (introduction, chapters on liberal meritocratic capitalism, homoploutia, assortative mating). Subscribe to Branko Milanovic's Global Inequality and More 3.0 substack. **Milestone**: write a 500-word piece on whether the UK exhibits homoploutia and what data would test it.

**Week 5 — The UK political-economy backbone.** Read Brett Christophers's *Rentier Capitalism* (2020). Begin Resolution Foundation's *Top of the Charts* weekly. Listen to two Macrodose episodes and two Odd Lots episodes; subscribe to both feeds. **Milestone**: map Christophers's seven rent categories onto current UK industries.

**Week 6 — Wilkinson and Pickett (critically) plus Stiglitz.** Read *The Spirit Level* (2009) alongside Snowdon's *The Spirit Level Delusion* (2010) critique. Read Stiglitz's *The Price of Inequality* (2012) chapters 1–4. Listen to relevant Past Present Future and These Times episodes. **Milestone**: write a balanced 1,000-word essay assessing whether the Wilkinson–Pickett correlations support the strong causal claims.

### Phase 2 — UK policy depth (weeks 7–12)

The aim is to know more about UK wealth taxation than 99% of public commentators, with primary-source command of the Wealth Tax Commission, CenTax, IPPR and Resolution Foundation evidence.

**Week 7 — The Wealth Tax Commission.** Read the Wealth Tax Commission's final report (December 2020) and the executive summaries of the 50+ background evidence papers at wealthandpolicy.com. Focus on Advani–Hughson–Tarrant (revenue modelling), Burgherr (administrative costs), Daly–Hughson–Loutzenhiser (liquidity), and Chamberlain (trust handling). **Milestone**: derive the £260bn (£500k threshold) and £80bn (£2m threshold) revenue figures yourself from the model assumptions.

**Week 8 — UK tax history and CenTax research.** Read Advani–Hughson–Summers's "How much tax do the rich really pay?" (*ORXP* 2023). Read Advani–Burgherr–Summers's non-dom papers (CAGE 2022; *Economic Journal* 2025). Read Glennerster's 2012 *Journal of Social Policy* paper on the 1974 wealth-tax failure. **Milestone**: produce a timeline of UK wealth-related tax reforms 1965–2026 with revenue implications for each.

**Week 9 — Counterarguments and the European experience.** Read the OECD 2018 *Role and Design of Net Wealth Taxes*. Read Brülhart–Gruber–Krapf–Schmidheiny (*AEJ:EP* 2022) on Switzerland and Jakobsen–Jakobsen–Kleven–Zucman (2020) on Denmark. Read Spanish solidarity-tax analysis from the EU Tax Observatory. Listen to two EconTalk episodes on taxation as steelman. **Milestone**: write a 1,500-word "Best Case Against a UK Wealth Tax" essay, then write the rebuttal.

**Week 10 — Housing and land.** Read Josh Ryan-Collins's *Why Can't You Afford a Home?* (2018). Read Brett Christophers's *Our Lives in Their Portfolios* (2023). Read Mulheirn's blog posts on UK housing supply versus demand, and the Shelter/Resolution Foundation responses. **Milestone**: produce a one-page assessment of the supply-vs-demand UK housing debate with your own provisional position and the evidence that would change it; build a stock-flow diagram of the UK housing-finance nexus.

**Week 11 — Financialisation and rentier capitalism.** Read Adair Turner's *Between Debt and the Devil* (2015) chapters 1–7. Read Ann Pettifor's *The Production of Money* (2017). Skim Grace Blakeley's *Vulture Capitalism* (2024) and Rana Foroohar's *Homecoming* (2022). Subscribe to Adam Tooze's Chartbook. **Milestone**: explain endogenous money creation in three accessible paragraphs.

**Week 12 — Phase 1–2 synthesis week.** No new books. Re-read your own notes from weeks 1–11. Write a 3,000-word essay: "How the UK became as unequal as it is, and what would change that — a synthesis." Test it on a non-economist friend. **Milestone**: complete the essay; identify three areas where you have residual uncertainty and three where you have a strong, defensible view.

### Phase 3 — Technical fluency (weeks 13–18)

The aim is to be able to compute, replicate and critique distributional statistics yourself, with command of the central methodological controversies.

**Week 13 — CORE Econ technical core.** Work through CORE Econ ESPP units 5–10 (inequality, equilibrium, banks and credit, asymmetric information). Begin the CORE "Doing Economics" Project 5 (Lorenz/Gini in Python). **Milestone**: replicate a UK Lorenz curve from FRS or WAS data in Python or R; compute the Gini, Palma, decile ratios, and top shares on a toy dataset.

**Week 14 — Inequality measurement theory.** Read Frank Cowell's *Measuring Inequality* (3rd ed., free at darp.lse.ac.uk/MI3) chapters 1–5. Re-read Atkinson 1970 *JET* alongside Cowell's treatment. **Milestone**: write out the axiomatic foundations of the Gini, Atkinson and Generalised Entropy classes, and prove (or sketch) the Bourguignon decomposability result.

**Week 15 — Causal inference for inequality.** Work through Angrist–Pischke's *Mostly Harmless Econometrics* chapters 3–6 (OLS as conditional expectation, IV, panel/FE, DiD). Read Card–Krueger 1994 *AER* alongside the Neumark–Wascher critique and the Cengiz–Dube–Lindner–Zipperer *QJE* 2019 meta-analysis. **Milestone**: write a methodological note on what we know about minimum wage employment effects.

**Week 16 — WID and DINA in practice.** Read the 2025 DINA Guidelines. Read Piketty–Saez–Zucman's 2018 *QJE* "Distributional National Accounts." Install the `wid` R package or the WID Python equivalent; pull UK top 1% income share time series. Replicate one of the WID figures from raw inputs using Blanchet's `gpinter` generalised Pareto interpolation. **Milestone**: produce a UK top 1% income share chart 1918–2024 with confidence in your data lineage.

**Week 17 — The methodological wars.** Read Saez–Zucman (*QJE* 2016) on US wealth concentration. Read Auten–Splinter (*JPE* 2024). Read the Piketty–Saez–Zucman 2024 rebuttal note (gabriel-zucman.eu/files/PSZ2024.pdf) and the Auten–Splinter reply. Read Smith–Zidar–Zwick (*QJE* 2023) on heterogeneous returns. Read NBER WP 33678 (Auerbach–Burman–Reck–Iselin 2025) for the synthesis attempt. **Milestone**: write a 2,000-word piece — "Why the top 1% income share is contested, and what difference it makes for UK debate."

**Week 18 — Chetty and the Opportunity Atlas.** Watch the Raj Chetty Ec 50 lecture videos (opportunityinsights.org/course) at 1.5×; work through two problem sets. Read the 2014 *QJE* "Where is the Land of Opportunity?" paper and the 2017 *Science* "Fading American Dream" paper. **Milestone**: produce a one-page comparison of US and UK intergenerational mobility evidence and what UK data infrastructure would be needed to replicate Chetty's geographic decomposition in the UK.

### Phase 4 — Synthesis and original contribution (weeks 19–26)

The aim is to convert reading into original output. By the end, you should have a draft research project, a public-facing writing portfolio, and ongoing source diet that compounds.

**Week 19 — Game theory of taxation: foundations.** Re-read Allingham–Sandmo (1972). Read Kleven–Knudsen–Kreiner–Pedersen–Saez (*Econometrica* 2011). Read Alstadsæter–Johannesen–Zucman (*AER* 2019). Read Advani–Burgherr–Summers (*EJ* 2025). Read Saez (2001) and Diamond–Saez (2011 *JEP*) on optimal income taxation. **Milestone**: write a 1,500-word piece identifying three places where game-theoretic reasoning is implicit-but-undeveloped in the UK wealth-tax literature.

**Week 20 — Mechanism design and the COST option.** Read Posner–Weyl's *Radical Markets* (2018) chapter 1. Read the critiques (Clancy, Cowen, the law-and-economics literature on Harberger taxes). Re-read the Wealth Tax Commission's valuation evidence papers. **Milestone**: outline a paper "Self-Assessed Wealth Taxation: A COST-based Mechanism for the UK" — research question, methods, proposed empirical calibration.

**Week 21 — Yard-sale models and wealth condensation.** Read Boghosian's 2014 *Physical Review E* paper and his 2017 *Physica A* "Oligarchy as a Phase Transition." Read the 2025 networked-extension arXiv paper. Read Cardoso–Gonçalves–Iglesias (2023). **Milestone**: outline a second paper — "Phase-Transition Calibration of Yard-Sale Models to UK Wealth Microdata."

**Week 22 — Networks, homophily and mobility.** Read Matthew Jackson's *The Human Network* (2019) selectively; read his 2025 arXiv:2506.13016 paper. Read Ben Ansell's "Six provocations on the origins and impacts of the UK housing emergency" (2025). Read selected INET Oxford working papers (Wiese et al. 2024; Bavaro–Boscolo–Tedeschi 2024). **Milestone**: identify which of your six potential original contributions has the strongest fit with your existing publication record; produce a 2-page research proposal.

**Week 23 — Technology, automation, work.** Read Daniel Susskind's *A World Without Work* (2020) and Aaron Benanav's *Automation and the Future of Work* (2020) as a paired set. Read Acemoglu–Johnson's *Power and Progress* (2023) chapters 1–3 and the chapters on AI. Read the tech-inequality block (Zuboff, Noble, Liu) if not done earlier. **Milestone**: write a 1,500-word piece on whether AI will reduce or amplify UK wealth inequality, taking a defensible position.

**Week 24 — International comparison deep dive.** Read the EU Tax Observatory's *Global Tax Evasion Report 2024*. Read the Spanish solidarity-tax evaluation literature. Read the Norwegian wealth-tax emigration debate (Dagens Næringsliv reports plus the academic critiques). Read the French ISF-to-IFI reform analysis from the Institut des Politiques Publiques. **Milestone**: produce a comparative table — design features, revenue, behavioural responses, political durability — for the Swiss, Norwegian, Spanish, French and proposed UK wealth-tax regimes.

**Week 25 — Public-facing output.** No new substantive reading. Produce three public-facing outputs: (1) a Substack/Medium piece (~2,500 words) on "What I'd actually do about UK wealth inequality" reflecting your synthesised view; (2) a 600-word op-ed-style version of the same argument; (3) a Twitter/Bluesky thread version (15–20 posts) summarising the case. Test all three on three different audiences: an economist, a policy-engaged generalist, and a non-economics friend. **Milestone**: publish at least one of the three. The credibility move is to show your workings: concepts, data choices, caveats, and where the strongest counterarguments bite. That is the point at which you stop being "inspired by Gary Stevenson" and start being legible as a researcher in your own right.

**Week 26 — Plan the next six months.** Review your reading log, your written outputs and your research-proposal drafts. Identify: which book or course from this curriculum delivered the most value relative to time invested; which area you would now go *deeper* on if you had another six months; which research project from the Tier 4 game-theory section you would actually pursue and what the next concrete step is (e.g., contact Arun Advani at CenTax; contact INET Oxford; submit a short paper to Wealth Tax Commission Working Papers; apply for a part-time MSc or doctoral position). **Milestone**: a written 12-month plan covering July 2026 to June 2027, plus an introduction email sent to at least one UK academic in this field.

### How to use the plan

Three points of operational advice.

**First**, ten hours per week is genuinely feasible only if you protect it. The most reliable structure is ~2 hours per weekday morning and ~3 hours on Saturday, with Sunday left fallow. Books read faster on commutes; papers and technical work need desk time.

**Second**, do not skip the milestone outputs. The act of writing forces synthesis; the act of testing on non-economist friends reveals where your understanding is brittle. By Week 12 you should have ~10,000 words of your own writing on UK inequality; by Week 26, ~20,000 words.

**Third**, this curriculum is deliberately heavier on the canon and the UK policy fight than on quantitative methods, on the judgement that public-facing credibility — your stated weighting — needs both empirical grounding and policy command more than it needs advanced econometric technique. If you want the quantitative-foundations weighting heavier, swap Tier 2 housing/financialisation reading for additional MIT 14.661 lectures and the Cameron–Trivedi advanced chapters in weeks 11–12, and move the Cowell/Auten–Splinter weeks earlier.

## Conclusion — what this curriculum will give you

If you complete this plan, by July 2026 you will have read or substantively skimmed approximately twenty books, fifty academic papers and four hundred policy reports/articles; you will be able to compute UK inequality statistics independently from WAS, FRS and WID data; you will know more about the Wealth Tax Commission's evidence base than the median Treasury official; you will hold defensible views on contested questions (Auten–Splinter vs PSZ, Mulheirn vs Resolution Foundation, the wealth-tax design question) rather than parroting them.

More importantly, you will have **identified a distinctive intellectual lane** — game-theoretic mechanism design for UK wealth-tax valuation; yard-sale wealth-condensation models calibrated to UK data; coalition-game analysis of UK tax politics — where your existing research credentials in multi-agent cooperation give you a genuine comparative advantage that almost nobody else in UK inequality discourse holds. This is the most credible path to producing original work rather than repeating others' arguments.

Two warnings.

**First, beware the Gary Stevenson trap.** Stevenson is an excellent populariser whose framing of UK distributional macro is internally consistent and politically effective. He is also operating in a different register than rigorous academic work; his policy specifics are sometimes loose, his data citations occasionally imprecise. Use him as inspiration for *audience-finding* and *framing*, not as a methodological model. The 4 May 2025 *Decoding the Gurus* critique is worth listening to.

**Second, beware political tribalism.** The UK inequality literature splits into roughly three camps — mainstream-IFS-Resolution-Foundation, heterodox-NEF-TJN-Murphy, and academic-Marxist-Christophers-Blakeley — and your reading list deliberately samples all three. Resist premature affiliation. The Wealth Tax Commission and the EU Tax Observatory matter more for credibility than positioning within any single tradition.

What this curriculum cannot give you is institutional standing, and that is the next question. By Week 26 you should be in a position to ask whether you want to remain an independent advocate (the Gary Stevenson path), seek an applied research position at an institution like CenTax or INET Oxford, pursue a part-time doctoral programme at LSE III or Warwick CAGE, or attempt a hybrid (e.g., a Substack-plus-research-affiliation model on the Adam Tooze pattern). Your existing Springer publication on game theory and multi-agent cooperation, combined with the original-contribution work outlined in the game-theory section, gives you a more credible academic on-ramp than most career-switchers possess. The question is whether you want to take it.

Either way, the path to producing genuinely original work on UK wealth inequality runs through the canon first. **Start with Piketty next week.**
