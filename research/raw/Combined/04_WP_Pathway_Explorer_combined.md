# Pathway Explorer — A Widening Participation Technology Proposal for London

**Build a single tool that takes a postcode and returns the things students, parents, teachers and your own WP staff currently piece together by hand from a dozen PDFs and spreadsheets.** The opportunity is concrete: no public UK product combines area context (POLAR4 / TUNDRA / IMD), provider-level destinations, LEO earnings filtered by background, bursary eligibility, and a cross-scheme WP programme finder in one student-facing surface. The OfS data is technically open but practically fragmented; HEAT serves staff but has no student-facing portal; Zero Gravity is gated and commercial; Discover Uni is course-level only; UCAS's new (December 2025) scholarships search is funding-only with 789 live opportunities at launch but no postcode logic. A standalone Python/FastAPI + Vue 3 build is achievable in a weekend (read-only postcode lookup), demonstrable as a 3-month MVP with real APP-aligned analytics, and credible as a 6–12 month funded product that plugs into the TASO/HEAT-led Higher Education Evaluation Library (HEEL) launching in 2026 and your WealthLens UK inequality work. This brief gives you scopes, architecture, datasets with URLs, risks, funding routes, an internal pitch, and an honest list of what cannot yet be done — everything you need to walk into your team meeting next week.

## Why this is the right gap to fill

Practitioners and students hit the same wall from opposite sides. A Year 12 in Newham can find their POLAR4 quintile on the OfS postcode tool, then has to leave it and visit Discover Uni for earnings, then UCAS for courses, then each university website for contextual offers, then UCAS's scholarships search or Blackbullion (if their university subscribes) or charity sites for bursaries, then Sutton Trust / Brilliant Club / Zero Gravity / IntoUniversity / upReach / Aimhigher London individually to test programme eligibility. Your WP team simultaneously runs Eventbrite for sign-ups, HEAT for longitudinal tracking, Excel for cohort lists, Power BI for APP reporting, and bespoke postcode checkers on the public site — five tools where one consented data flow would suffice. **The OfS Equality of Opportunity Risk Register (EORR) makes "information and guidance" Risk 2 — your tool is a direct response to a named regulatory risk.** And OfS itself explicitly warns that its postcode lookup is not designed to determine bursary or contextual-offer eligibility on its own — yet universities and even some scholarships routinely send students there. That mismatch between what the public tool can safely do and what students actually need it to do is precisely the user-experience gap to close.

Critically, the data layer to fix this is already open under OGL v3 and CC BY: postcodes.io (postcode → LSOA), MHCLG IMD 2019 (LSOA → deprivation decile), the OfS postcode CSV (POLAR4/TUNDRA/ABCS — the February 2026 release is a 186 MB file), the DfE Explore Education Statistics API (LEO earnings by FSM × POLAR × provider × subject at sector level, though *not yet* by FSM at provider-by-subject level), GIAS (school directory with FSM%), and the Discover Uni dataset (NSS, Graduate Outcomes, continuation, tariff). The novel work is **structuring the unstructured** — university contextual-offer policies, bursary criteria, and WP-programme eligibility — into a JSON schema and exposing it through one API.

## The public data landscape

For England, the DfE's **Widening participation in higher education** release tracks progression to HE by age 19 for state-funded pupils using matched NPD, ILR and HESA data, with breakdowns by FSM, disadvantage, gender, ethnicity, SEND, first language, children in need, looked-after, care leavers, local authority, region, school type and POLAR — including progression to high-tariff providers. Its methodology is also important: it only tracks entry by age 19, does not control for prior attainment, and is an estimate due to matching procedures. **OfS** publishes the provider-facing infrastructure — the Access and Participation dashboard covering the full lifecycle (access, continuation, completion, attainment, progression) — and explicitly tells providers to use it alongside the EORR and to make their plans more accessible to prospective students and parents. **UCAS** fills several niches: the Multiple Equality Measure (MEM, combining sex, ethnicity, geography, school type and FSM into a composite entry-probability), end-of-cycle data, contextual-admissions guidance, the Outreach Connection Service, the Outreach Evaluator, and the new scholarships/bursaries/grants search. **Discover Uni** and **DfE LEO** cover outcomes — course-level and provider-level earnings, with 3- and 5-year LEO data. The **Social Mobility Commission** now publishes HE entry and earnings statistics by socio-economic background, which keeps the access story connected to labour-market outcomes. **HESA** remains the backbone for student records and Discover Uni, and has moved its Socioeconomic Index for Small Areas (SEISA) into official-statistics status — a sign the sector is moving toward more refined deprivation measures.

## What POLAR4 and TUNDRA do well — and where they fail

POLAR4 is simple, UK-wide and historically embedded — its main strengths are comparability and familiarity. Its main weaknesses are age and coarseness: the current classification is built using entrants from 2009–10 to 2013–14 on 2011 geographies, so it is not a real-time measure of current neighbourhood opportunity. **TUNDRA** is the OfS's improvement: England-only, individualised tracking from key stage 4 to HE, restricted to pupils in state-funded mainstream schools. That restriction is exactly why many WP practitioners prefer it — it reveals disadvantaged state-school pockets that broader neighbourhood averages can hide. OfS now explicitly recommends TUNDRA wherever possible.

But the crucial conceptual limit applies to both: **POLAR and TUNDRA measure the likelihood of HE participation in an area, not individual socio-economic disadvantage.** A postcode in quintile 1 does not automatically mean a young person is poor; a young person in quintile 5 may still face substantial disadvantage. This is why UCAS built MEM, why HESA is developing SEISA, and why universities layer postcode flags with FSM, school context, care/refugee status and other applicant-level indicators. The dominant academic critique (Boliver; Sutton Trust 2021; UCL WP Group 2022) is the ecological fallacy — affluent households inside Q1 MSOAs get flagged, and vice versa.

The design implication is decisive: **never present quintiles as labels on the student.** Present them as information about *their area's history of HE participation* and *what universities have committed to do about it*. Use framing like "Here are 12 universities that have published a contextual offer for someone in your area," not "You are a WP student." Co-design copy with student panels — Brilliant Club's Parent Power model is the template.

## What tools exist and what is missing

There are now useful public tools, but they cover different parts of the decision journey: OfS postcode lookup (POLAR/TUNDRA/Adult HE); Discover Uni (course-level outcomes, earnings, student experience); UCAS (entry requirements, contextual guidance, scholarships search from Dec 2025); HEAT (provider-side longitudinal tracking); UCAS Outreach Connection Service and Outreach Evaluator (adviser/provider tools). **The combined learner-facing postcode-to-options tool does not appear to exist as a mature public UK product** — one where a student enters a postcode and immediately sees area participation, likely destinations, earnings information *and* bursaries in one place. The OfS tool is one-postcode-at-a-time and designed primarily for staff; Discover Uni is course-organised, not geography-organised; UCAS's scholarships tool is funding-only; the equality explorers have been intermittently unavailable.

A second major gap is a **trustworthy low-income ROI explorer**. DfE's provider-level LEO dashboard can filter by provider, subject, sex, ethnicity, prior attainment and POLAR4, but it does **not** currently expose FSM at provider × subject level — only at sector level, where FSM graduates have lower median earnings and are nearly twice as likely to have no sustained destination five years on. Any institution building a "low-income ROI" tool now must either use proxies, secure restricted data, or be very explicit about limits. **This is a hard "do not lead with this" warning**: a misleading ROI ranking is worse than no ranking.

A third gap is **evidence-sharing**. TASO's toolkit ratings are honest about uncertainty: pre-entry IAG is low-cost / medium-evidence / mixed-impact; mentoring is medium-cost / emerging-evidence / small positive effects on aspirations; summer schools are high-cost / emerging-evidence / more research needed on behaviour. Encouraging tech examples exist (UCAS Outreach Connection Service; Brightside online mentoring; Sussex's transition programme for contextual-offer students; international NBER work showing text-message interventions can support application and transition steps at scale in low-income school populations). But the standardised infrastructure to test which interventions work at scale is largely missing.

## What to build: Pathway Explorer plus a practitioner mirror

The product has two faces sharing one backend. The **student/parent-facing Pathway Explorer** takes a postcode (and optionally school URN, year group, intended subjects, FSM status, care/estranged/refugee flags) and returns:

- *Area context* — POLAR4, TUNDRA, IMD decile, IDACI (separately for under-16s), the dominant secondary school for that postcode with its FSM% from GIAS — framed as information about the area, not the student.
- *Contextually-matched universities* — providers whose published contextual-admissions policies the student would meet, with entry tariffs and continuation rates.
- *Earnings, with strong caveats* — LEO median earnings for similar students by subject and provider, explicitly noting which filters are FSM-aware (sector-level only) versus POLAR/provider-level (more granular but not low-income-specific). No "ROI ranking" headline; instead, an "earnings and outcomes explorer" with clear notes about uncertainty, subject mix, local labour markets and student-finance assumptions.
- *Bursaries* — funding the student would be eligible for at each of those universities, starting from your own institution and then external/UCAS-linked opportunities.
- *Cross-scheme WP programmes* — Sutton Trust Pathways/Summer Schools, Brilliant Club Scholars, Zero Gravity, Causeway, IntoUniversity, upReach, your own summer schools, Aimhigher London — with deadlines, eligibility logic, and direct apply links.
- *"What next" action card* — concrete next step, not a recommendation.

The **WP team dashboard** uses the same backend to track demographic reach against APP targets, flag under-served wards within your catchment, log event engagement via QR-code check-in with real-time POLAR/IMD enrichment, capture consent and parental-permission state, and export to HEAT (CSV), Power BI, and the future TASO HEEL submission format. The three concrete jobs it serves: **targeting** (upload a school/postcode list and enrich it instantly with TUNDRA/POLAR/FSM proxies, local progression rates and prior institutional engagement); **delivery support** (workshop handouts and follow-up links tailored to a cohort, with bursary and contextual-admissions info baked in); **evaluation** (activity exposure logging, TASO-aligned pre/post measures, MOAT classification, HEAT and HEEL-ready exports). **Position the tool as HEAT-complementary, not HEAT-replacing** — HEAT keeps the sector longitudinal record; your tool fixes the event-day and student-facing layer HEAT was never built for.

## The weekend prototype: a single honest postcode lookup

Scope ruthlessly. In a weekend you build **"Postcode → Pathway Snapshot"**: one Vue 3 page with a postcode input that returns a single shareable card. The card shows POLAR4 quintile, TUNDRA quintile, IMD decile (IDACI separately for under-16s), the dominant secondary school for that postcode with its FSM% from GIAS, and three contextually-matched London universities pulled from a hand-curated JSON file covering the 19 London providers' contextual policies. **Mock everything that cannot be done in 48 hours**: bursary data is a small static JSON, "eligible programmes" is a hardcoded list of five named schemes with simple rules, LEO is a single subject (Computing or Law, where the spread is most dramatic — c. £32k between top and bottom decile provider per LEO 2022–23) and clearly labelled as a sample. Backend is FastAPI with three endpoints (`/lookup/{postcode}`, `/providers`, `/programmes`); frontend is one Vue 3 SFC with a results panel and a "Show your WP team" share button. Deploy free on Fly.io or Railway. The demo line is: *"This took one weekend and uses only open data. Now imagine it with your APP cohort logic and HEAT integration."* That single artefact is what gets the team meeting on the calendar.

## The three-month MVP: real data, real cohorts, deployable

The MVP turns the demo into a production-grade tool you can put in front of a real outreach cohort. The student side gains the full postcode → contextual-offers matrix for **all 19 London universities plus the Russell Group** (a single-engineer scrape-and-structure exercise — contextual policies are publicly published, just in PDFs and web pages), live LEO data via the EES CSV-by-GUID API at the most granular level public data supports (with explicit caveats where FSM × provider × subject is not yet exposed), a bursary database covering the same providers (start with the structured financial-support spend lines in published APPs, supplemented by UCAS scholarships search data), and a WP-programme eligibility engine covering the eight or nine flagship national schemes plus Aimhigher London. Add a "save my pathway" feature with email-based magic links — no accounts, no minor-data risk.

The practitioner side becomes usable: a Vue 3 admin dashboard with QR-code event check-in (mobile-first), instant postcode enrichment on registration, consent and parental-permission capture aligned to your DPIA, cohort demographic breakdown against APP targets and the EORR's 12 risks, an APP-target progress view, and CSV/Excel export in HEAT's bulk-upload format. Add Theory of Change capture using TASO's templates and MOAT activity classification so every logged event is automatically tagged for Type 1 narrative reporting and ready for Type 2 empirical analysis later. **Deliverables at month three**: a public demo URL, a live internal staging instance with one consented cohort, a 10-page write-up against APP and EORR risks, a DPIA, and a published GitHub repo (MIT or AGPL).

## The 6–12 month funded build: a sector-credible product

With internal funding (HEIF, APP investment line, internal innovation/seedcorn — the fastest route) plus a small external grant, the 6–12 month vision extends in three directions. **Scope nationally**: all 140+ English HE providers' contextual policies and bursaries, structured as a queryable schema with a published JSON Schema others can contribute to; coverage of all four UK nations' deprivation indices (IMD 2019/2025, WIMD 2019, SIMD 2020, NIMDM 2017). **Evidence-grade evaluation**: built-in randomisation and treatment/control assignment infrastructure so the tool can host TASO-aligned Type 3 evaluations of digital outreach interventions — a documented sector gap TASO's Institutional Data Use Project explicitly flagged, and a place where the NBER digital-messaging evidence base offers a credible mechanism to test. **Sector integration**: a published HEAT export adapter, a TASO/HEEL submission feed (HEEL launches in 2026 and will need partners — a HEAT submission portal is already live for HEAT members), an embeddable widget for school websites and Unifrog, and a Discover Uni widget integration. Position the funded build as **infrastructure that reduces evaluation cost for every provider** — that framing aligns with how OfS is reshaping funding post-2025–26 Strategic Priorities Grant cut.

## Technical architecture you can defend in one slide

The stack is appropriate, defensible, and matches your skills. **Backend**: FastAPI with Pydantic models for every public schema (`Postcode`, `AreaContext`, `ProviderPolicy`, `Bursary`, `Programme`), async `httpx` for upstream calls, Redis (or SQLite for v1) cache for postcode lookups with TTLs by source (postcodes.io: 30 days; OfS postcode CSV: pinned by release date), Postgres with PostGIS for spatial queries, Alembic for migrations, pytest with property-based tests for the eligibility rules engine. **Frontend**: Vue 3 with `<script setup>`, Pinia for state, Vite, Tailwind for speed, Chart.js or D3 for the wealth/earnings pipeline visualisation that links to WealthLens UK, and `@vueuse/head` for SEO. **Data ingestion**: a small ETL package (Prefect or even a cron'd Python script) that pulls EES CSV-by-GUID for LEO, refreshes the OfS postcode CSV on each annual release, refreshes IMD when MHCLG updates it, and re-scrapes contextual policies quarterly with human-in-the-loop validation. **Hosting**: Fly.io or Hetzner for cost; Cloudflare in front; everything containerised; CI in GitHub Actions; observability via Sentry. **Auth**: minimal — public side anonymous with optional magic-link save; staff side SSO via your university's Azure AD/Entra. **Eligibility engine**: implement as declarative JSON-Logic or a small DSL so non-engineers in the WP team can maintain rules — this is the single highest-leverage architectural decision; keep eligibility logic *out of code*. The data model is **geography-first** (postcode → LSOA/MSOA/local-authority) so the same base feeds WealthLens UK; event entities (intervention, cohort, exposure, outcome) are structured for HEAT export from day one.

## The data layer with URLs you can paste into a sprint plan

Postcode geocoding uses **postcodes.io** (`https://api.postcodes.io/postcodes/{postcode}`, MIT, no key). Area classifications come from the OfS postcode CSV at `officeforstudents.org.uk/data-and-analysis/young-participation-by-area/get-the-postcode-data/` (refreshed February 2026, 186 MB — POLAR4, TUNDRA MSOA, TUNDRA LSOA, ABCS quintiles). IMD 2019 deciles ship from `imd-by-postcode.opendatacommunities.org` or the raw LSOA file at `gov.uk/government/statistics/english-indices-of-deprivation-2019`; check whether the IMD 2025 update is now the authoritative version. LEO comes from Explore Education Statistics using permanent CSV-by-GUID URLs — `explore-education-statistics.service.gov.uk/find-statistics/leo-graduate-and-postgraduate-outcomes/2022-23` and the provider-level companion. School data lives in GIAS (`get-information-schools.service.gov.uk`, public API) and Compare School Performance. Course-level data comes from the Discover Uni dataset ZIP at `hesa.ac.uk/support/tools-and-downloads/unistats` (CC BY 4.0, weekly updates). The OfS Access and Participation data dashboard bulk CSVs at `officeforstudents.org.uk/data-and-analysis/access-and-participation-data-dashboard/about-the-data-dashboard/get-the-data/` underpin the staff-side APP target view; the EORR taxonomy comes from the same site. The DfE *Widening participation in higher education* release is the canonical source for FSM/SEND/care/POLAR breakdowns of progression. The Social Mobility Commission's HE-entry and returns-to-education statistics live on their site and are essential for the inequality framing. **Watch-outs**: postcode quintile reuse is frozen by OfS to preserve consistency; NI postcodes are LPS-licensed and not redistributable, so v1 explicitly scopes England-only with Wales/Scotland/NI as roadmap items; HEAT integration requires a paid membership conversation with the University of Kent team that operates it.

## Risks, ethics and GDPR — the section that gets it past Information Governance

Three risks dominate and each has a clean mitigation.

**Minors and consent.** Anyone under 13 is high-risk under GDPR Article 8 and the ICO Children's Code. Mitigation: the public Pathway Explorer takes no personal identifiers, just postcode and self-reported context, and stores nothing without an explicit magic-link save (over-13 only, with an age-gate page and parental-permission flow for the WP team's tracked cohorts). All staff-side tracking flows through a DPIA you complete with your DPO before the MVP goes live; lawful basis is Public Task (Article 6(1)(e)) tied to the OfS APP statutory duty, with consent layered on top for parental permission of under-16s in school programmes. Apply data minimisation aggressively: never store full postcode for tracked individuals — store LSOA only — and never store DOB, only year-of-birth or year-group.

**Deficit framing.** Telling a Year 11 they live in a POLAR Q1 area can either expand their horizons or pathologise them. Mitigation: never label the student; label the area's history, and pair every quintile with a concrete action ("Here are universities that have published a contextual offer for someone in your area"). Co-design copy with student panels. Audit-test the language against the OfS's own caution that POLAR/TUNDRA are *participation* measures, not poverty measures, and against the academic critique of ecological fallacy.

**Algorithmic eligibility.** An eligibility engine that wrongly tells a student they are not eligible for, say, Sutton Trust Pathways is potentially more harmful than no tool. Mitigation: **never close doors.** The engine returns "likely eligible / possibly eligible / cannot confirm — apply directly" with explicit links and an explanation of the limiting criterion. Log every "not eligible" outcome for human review. Publish the rules as open JSON. Add a "tell us we got this wrong" feedback loop reviewed weekly during MVP. The same logic applies to the earnings explorer: surface DfE's own caveats that published provider outcomes are raw, uncontrolled results.

Additional considerations: full WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility from day one (your audience disproportionately includes disabled learners; also a legal requirement for public-sector bodies); a published privacy notice in plain English tested with Year 10s; a documented data retention schedule; supplier due diligence on any third-party (Sentry, Cloudflare, postcodes.io — self-hostable, removing that risk); an algorithmic transparency record published alongside the tool.

## How WP data connects to inequality — the WealthLens UK link

The same datasets power both projects. IMD, LEO, ONS Wealth and Assets Survey, ASHE, FRS and Understanding Society sit underneath WealthLens UK's wealth-inequality story and Pathway Explorer's WP story. **The visualisation argument is the connective tissue**: educational inequality is not a separate problem from wealth inequality — it is the *first valve* in a multi-stage funnel. Each stage is quantifiable and each has a public source:

- The SMC's 2024 figures: 45.6% of 18–20-year-olds from higher professional backgrounds are enrolled in HE, versus 25.2% from lower working-class backgrounds. The gap has narrowed but remains large.
- A Year 11 FSM pupil is roughly 60% as likely to enter HE as a non-FSM peer (DfE WP release).
- Per DfE LEO 2022–23, FSM graduates have lower median earnings five years out and are nearly twice as likely to have no sustained destination.
- The SMC's returns analysis: first degrees are associated with c. 27% earnings above the baseline group, but holding qualifications constant, 25–29-year-olds from higher professional backgrounds still earn 19% more than those from lower working-class backgrounds.
- The class pay gap inside elite occupations is c. 16% (Friedman & Laurison).
- IFS's HE work: disadvantaged students see some of the largest financial benefits from going to university, but they are less likely to attend, and even among students with the same grades on the same programme they still earn less on average later on.
- IFS's 2026 wealth recalculation: degree-holders' median wealth £420k vs £66k for those with no qualifications (2020–22).
- ONS WAS: median household wealth in Great Britain £293,700 (Apr 2020–Mar 2022); wealth Gini 0.59 versus 0.36 for disposable income; the top 1% of households hold the same share of wealth as the bottom 50% combined.
- Resolution Foundation: inheritances have roughly doubled over 20 years and flow disproportionately to the top quintile.

**Build the Pathway Explorer with a sister visualisation — a Sankey or cumulative-wealth-by-stage chart — that compounds these statistics into a single picture of how WP access at 18 maps to wealth distribution at 60.** That is the artefact that turns a postcode tool into a piece of public-interest infrastructure, and it is the framing that wins philanthropic funding. Universities UK's own London Economics work claims £14 of economic benefit per £1 of public HE investment; IFS estimates the average exchequer return per graduate at £100k+. **WP is not charity, it is the highest-NPV social investment the state makes** — that is the line that gets you in front of senior stakeholders. The Sutton Trust Opportunity Index is the closest existing analogue and a useful pointer for what good geography-first inequality visualisation can look like.

## How to pitch it internally next week

Walk in with three artefacts: the live weekend prototype (a URL on a phone), a one-page brief that maps the tool to OfS EORR Risks 1, 2, 4, 7 and 12, and a costed three-month plan with named deliverables. Open with the user story: a Year 12 in Tower Hamlets asking which universities she should consider, what she would earn, what bursaries she could get, and which programmes she could apply to today — and the seven tabs she currently has open. Show the prototype answering that on one page. Then pivot to the staff view: APP target progress, real-time demographic reach, QR-code event check-in, HEAT-compatible export. **Make the ask small**: a named WP team sponsor, one hour of the Information Governance team for an early DPIA, and access to one cohort for a controlled pilot in Term 2. Senior stakeholders care about regulatory risk, evaluation evidence, and student numbers — frame benefits in those terms, not in features. Close with the WealthLens UK Sankey: this is not a CRM, it is the access end of a wealth-inequality pipeline, and the university gets to be visibly building infrastructure for both.

A sensible first-year evaluation frame: reduced staff time spent assembling postcode and funding information; increased student awareness of bursaries and contextual support; increased click-through from workshops into sign-ups or follow-up guidance; and higher completion of target next steps (bursary applications, event attendance, adviser bookings). The honest claim is that the product improves **the timing, personalisation and measurability of IAG** — not that better information alone solves access. TASO's mixed evidence on pre-entry IAG demands that honesty.

## Open questions and limitations

Three things this tool cannot yet do, and you should say so out loud.

**Destination modelling.** There is no public official dataset that cleanly powers a postcode-to-"universities people from your area usually attend" recommender at the granularity a student would expect. A probabilistic model built from coarser public data may be feasible; a clean version would require a data-sharing agreement using restricted administrative data.

**Low-income ROI precision.** Public sources let you compare provider/subject outcomes and let you compare FSM versus non-FSM outcomes at sector level. They do not currently provide a public provider-by-subject-by-FSM returns product. Any tool that implies that level of certainty without restricted data risks false precision — and is more harmful than helpful.

**Coverage mismatch.** The most useful access data is England-only, state-school-based and age-19-focused; UCAS data covers most but not all UK full-time undergraduate provision; area measures are not the same as individual disadvantage. The product can still be highly useful, but it must explain those caveats clearly — in plain English, on the tool itself — rather than hiding them.

## Conclusion: the build is small, the framing is big

Almost everything you need to ship a credible v1 exists and is open: postcodes.io for geography, OfS for area classifications, EES for LEO, GIAS for schools, Discover Uni for courses, MHCLG for IMD, SMC for the inequality numbers. The genuinely novel work is **a structured eligibility schema** for contextual offers, bursaries and WP programmes — a single-engineer, three-month exercise. The strategic insight is that this is not a new CRM (HEAT exists), not a new mentoring platform (Zero Gravity and Brightside exist), and not a new course tool (Discover Uni exists). It is the *missing connective layer* between them, and the OfS Equality of Opportunity Risk Register has effectively pre-written its business case. Build the weekend prototype this Saturday, demo it to the WP team on Tuesday, file the DPIA in week two, and you have a defensible path to internal funding by month three, an MVP serving real cohorts by month six, and a sector-credible HEEL partner by month twelve — all while feeding the same data spine that powers your WealthLens UK story about how unequal valves at age 18 compound into unequal wealth at age 60.
