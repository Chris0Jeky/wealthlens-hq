# Engineer turned evidence-broker: a 90-day rebrand playbook

The fastest path from a London trading-systems engineer to a credible public voice on economic justice runs through **Bluesky and LinkedIn first, a Ghost blog as the owned home, and one Show HN civic-data tool as the credentialling moment** — not through trying to sound like an economist. The opportunity is unusually well-shaped: the British economic-justice conversation is hungry for technically literate voices that aren't think-tank insiders, the data-journalism niche rewards reproducibility (which engineers can do better than journalists), and the Springer SGAI-AI 2025 paper plus a finance-engineering CV is a near-unique credential stack. The risk is the mirror image: pretending to be an economist invites legitimate attack, while pretending the trading-systems day job doesn't exist invites worse attack. This report sets out a five-part operational plan — platforms, blog, events, narrative discipline, and a 90-day calendar — that uses the engineering identity as the warrant for the polemical work rather than apologising for it.

The dominant strategic move across every section is the same: **lead with the finding, never the topic; lead with the engineering, never the politics**. Both inversions are credibility-protecting and audience-expanding. Everything below operationalises them.

## 1. Platform strategy: where the audience actually is in 2026

**Bluesky and LinkedIn are the two platforms that matter for this rebrand. X is necessary but declining. TikTok is the high-upside experiment. Instagram is a deprioritise.** The reasoning runs through the audience composition, not the headline user numbers.

**LinkedIn is where the rebrand converts to opportunity** — speaking invitations, journalist DMs, advisory introductions, recruiter contact for civic-tech roles. The 2025 algorithm rewrite (the "360Brew" model) now scores profile-content alignment, so the headline and About section need to be rewritten around "engineer turning inequality data into public knowledge" before the first post lands; a misaligned profile throttles distribution by roughly half. Document-post carousels outperform every other format — Buffer's analysis of 4.8M posts found roughly 596% more engagement than text-only. The optimum for a 5-10 hour week is **two to three carousels per week, posted Tuesday–Thursday 10am–noon or 4–6pm UK time**, each unpacking a single chart from IFS/ONS/Resolution Foundation. External links cost roughly 60% of reach now and the "link in first comment" trick has been patched; post the substance natively and put any link in a follow-up comment if at all.

**Bluesky is where UK civic-tech and economic-justice discourse has actively migrated**. Pew found 43% of major news influencers now have Bluesky accounts (up from 21% pre-November 2024); JRF, NEF, IFS staff, ODI, mySociety, Full Fact, ProPublica and most of the FT data team are verified there via domain handles. Metricool's 2026 study shows engagement runs 2–4× higher per follower than X for accounts under 100K. Discovery happens through **Starter Packs and custom feeds**, not the algorithm — Cyd Harrell's civic-tech Starter Pack is the canonical entry point, and the Economics, Data Viz, UK Politics and CivicTech feeds will surface posts with the right hashtags. Verify the handle via a personal domain (`@yourname.co.uk`) within week one; this is the platform's de facto blue-check and costs nothing.

**X is necessary but no longer primary**. The post half-life is under two hours for sub-1K accounts, non-Premium reach is throttled, and the room is emptying. It remains the place where breaking economic news happens first and where UK journalists (Burn-Murdoch, Conway, Stevenson, Bell) still post — which means it's still where journalist discovery happens. The right posture is **one thread per week plus three to five substantive replies per day into a private List of 30–50 accounts**, not original-post-first. The reply-into-big-accounts strategy is by far the highest-ROI activity at the 0–1K stage: three thoughtful replies a day on Burn-Murdoch/Conway/IFS-grade accounts, each including a chart or primary source, conservatively yields 20–30 followers a day and 1,800–2,700 over 90 days.

**TikTok is the high-upside experiment** specifically because Gary Stevenson has proved the format works in the UK. His "Ilford post office worker's son → Citi top trader → inequality explainer" arc, scaled through Novara cross-posts, runs into the millions of views and is structurally identical to the engineering-without-privilege arc available here. Educational content gets ~82% viewer retention on the platform; 60–90-second talking-head plus screen-recorded chart explainers are the canonical format. The honest constraint: TikTok rewards volume more than other platforms, and at 5–10 hours per week the realistic cadence is one video per week batch-shot in a single session. Treat it as a 12-week experiment; if any one video clears 50K views, double down.

**Instagram is a deprioritise**. Mona Chalabi's 518K followers come from a hand-drawn-over-data signature visual style developed over a decade; without an equivalent moat it's not worth the capacity. A monthly carousel repost is fine.

### Accounts to study in depth, and what each one teaches

The five canonical figures the user asked about each model a different transferable technique. **Wendy Liu** demonstrates that one rebrandable polemical phrase ("Abolish Silicon Valley" began as a Twitter joke) does the work of a thousand posts — the lesson is to own a single sharp line, not a content calendar. **Timnit Gebru** demonstrates that polemic only works if every claim is bolted to a primary citation; her Stochastic Parrots paper is the warrant for every fight she's picked, and the SGAI-AI 2025 paper plays that role here. **Safiya Umoja Noble** demonstrates that an academic-adjacent voice operates through book and keynote circuit, with social as secondary — a useful long-horizon model. **mySociety** demonstrates that "useful" is itself a brand: project updates, open methodology, no Twitter fights. **ProPublica** demonstrates the "tweetstorm with receipts" format, where each major piece of analysis is treated as a self-contained thread rather than a teaser for a blog post.

### The 0-to-1,000 engaged X followers playbook

Pre-launch week: rewrite the bio to one specific line ("Engineer. Turning inequality data into public knowledge. Springer SGAI-AI 2025. ex-GE Digital."), pin a 8–10 tweet manifesto thread, and build a private List of 30–50 accounts (10 big — Burn-Murdoch, Conway, Stevenson, Criado Perez, IFS, ProPublica; 20 mid — JRF/ODI/NEF staff, individual academics, Bureau Local; 20 peers — early-career data journalists and civic-tech engineers). Daily rhythm: 15 minutes replying fast (within 30 minutes of original posts) on List members, 15 minutes on one substantive original post, 15 minutes on long-tail engagement and DMs. Weekly: one thread on Tuesday (UK policy day), 8–12 tweets each with a chart or primary source. Monthly: cross-post the best thread as a LinkedIn carousel and publish one blog deep-dive distributed as a thread, not as a teaser link. Realistic milestones: ~200 followers at day 30, ~500 at day 60, ~1,000 at day 90, with one thread clearing 200 likes and one "borrowed audience" moment (a journalist reply or podcast invite). **Bluesky will hit those numbers faster**; run them in parallel.

The four pitfalls that kill accounts in this niche are: looking preachy (every polemical claim must arrive with a source in the same tweet); getting pigeonholed (maintain at least three content pillars — proposed split: 40% wages/inequality data, 30% housing and assets, 30% tech labour and widening participation); picking fights with named individuals when you don't yet have institutional standing to back the polemic; and over-investing in X relative to Bluesky.

## 2. Blog and publishing strategy

**Host on Ghost (Pro at $9–25/month or self-hosted on Hetzner for ~$15/month all-in) at your own domain, with a Substack mirror for distribution.** The reasoning is threefold: anyone vetting your rebrand will visit the site, and a Ghost site on a personal domain signals craft and ownership where a Substack URL signals newsletter hobbyist; SEO matters disproportionately for evidence-led writing that you want findable two years later, and Substack ranks badly outside its own network; and interactive data is your edge, which means embedding Observable notebooks, D3, Datawrapper iframes — all trivial on Ghost, impossible on Substack. The Substack mirror with canonical URL pointing home captures the Notes/Recommendations discovery loop without lock-in. Use a clean theme (Solo or Edition), Plausible or Fathom for analytics (signals values), and a public GitHub repo for every post's data and notebook. Buttondown or Ghost's native newsletter for email. **The single highest-impact engineering habit is linking to the GitHub repo with data plus notebook from every post** — this one practice doubles the hit rate with technical newsletter curators and triples credibility with the HN audience.

### Hacker News strategy

The honest landscape: inequality and economic-policy posts regularly hit the HN front page, but with bimodal outcomes — either 300+ points with civil debate or quickly flagged. **The discriminator is methodology, not politics.** Recent front-page hits in this space include Burn-Murdoch's life-expectancy charts, multiple UK housing-affordability dashboards (HN items 42434658, 44554795, 46110908, 44555030 in 2024–25), "Quantifying the High-Frequency Trading Arms Race" (the QJE paper underpinning post 1), and various Show HN civic-data tools.

What works: rigour over conclusion (a post that says "I expected X, found Y, here's why I might still be wrong" beats a post that says "X is true"); reproducibility links in paragraph one; specific numbers in the title; original analysis rather than commentary on someone else's chart; technical novelty as a Trojan horse (a post titled "Backtesting a meritocracy" gets read by people who would never click "Is university admission fair?"); contrarian-but-evidenced framing that defends a counterintuitive finding against the author's own priors.

What kills posts: advocacy verbs in the title ("must," "should," "we need"); personal-essay framing ("Why I'm angry about…"); unsourced statistics in the first paragraph; raw counts not normalised to rates; politicians named in titles; anything that reads as primarily about identity.

Submission mechanics: post at 07:00–11:00 US Eastern (noon–4pm UK) on Tuesday, Wednesday or Thursday; submit, then go offline for 20 minutes and don't refresh; have two to three substantive comments drafted in advance addressing the most likely methodological objections; pin a top-level comment of your own linking to the GitHub repo with limitations section. Never ask friends to upvote — HN's vote-ring detection is mature. Failed posts can be re-submitted with a new title after roughly two weeks via HN's "second chance" pool. **The openpoverty-uk Show HN is the single highest-probability front-page event in the calendar — schedule it for week 8–10 when the brand is established and the README is polished, with a screenshot/GIF and an authored first comment explaining the why and the limitations.**

### CFP craft: three example outlines this user could submit immediately

The structural recipe is four blocks across 250–350 words: a two-sentence hook with a concrete UK-relevant inequity and a number; three to four sentences of evidence drawn from your particular vantage (trading systems, SGAI publication, code you've written); three to four sentences naming the new claim; two sentences on what the audience walks out able to do. Title craft: one concrete plus one abstract noun ("Latency and Class"), a reversed assumption ("Trading Systems Aren't Neutral"), a question demanding a specific answer ("Who Pays for Microsecond Markets?"), or an unexpected pairing ("From Order Books to Universal Credit"). Avoid "Towards a Framework for…," anything ending "in the Age of AI," any title with the verb "explore" or "reimagine."

**CFP example 1 — for AIES 2026 Global Inequalities track or FAccT 2027 CRAFT:** "What Trading System Engineers Know About Algorithmic Fairness That FAccT Doesn't." Argument: in equity markets, "best execution" is a regulated, audited, mathematically defined obligation; in UK Universal Credit, Home Office risk scoring, and DWP fraud detection, no equivalent exists. Proposes that financial-grade execution standards — observability, kill switches, post-trade analytics — are a more tractable governance template for public-sector AI than current algorithmic-auditing proposals. Delivers a taxonomy of seven trading-systems controls mapped to public-AI failure modes.

**CFP example 2 — for MozFest, ODI Friday Lectures, 5x15:** "The Spread." Walks through "spread" in three settings — equity markets, payday lending, Buy Now Pay Later — using public data and engineering experience. Reframes consumer financial harm as a market microstructure problem rather than a behavioural one. Ends with five concrete reforms HM Treasury could make tomorrow, written for a non-technical room.

**CFP example 3 — for IFGS 2027, Money 20/20 Europe Regulation track, The Conduit:** "Widening Participation Is an Infrastructure Problem." UK fintech inclusion strategy has been about apps; the harder story is the plumbing — KYC pipelines, payment rails, credit-bureau APIs — that decides who shows up as creditworthy at all. Engineer's tour of three real systems with the engineering reasons each excludes; three architectural patterns that make inclusion a default, borrowed from how trading platforms treat tail latency.

## 4. Storytelling discipline: voice, frame, and how to take a punch

The single most important narrative rule is that **personal story is scaffolding for structural argument, never the building itself**. The working ratio observed across Wendy Liu, James Plunkett, Faiza Shaheen, Brian Merchant and Caroline Criado Perez is roughly 10–20% personal content to 80–90% structural in any given piece; in a 2,000-word essay that is 200–400 words of "I" content, ideally front-loaded and used as anchor moments rather than dispersed throughout. The personal story credentials the speaker and provides illustrative texture; the evidence does the argument.

The "show don't tell" rules for the no-privilege-to-engineering origin story are concrete. Use specifics, never adjectives — "my mum did two jobs; the school computer suite was where I learned to code because we didn't have a working PC at home" rather than "I came from a poor background." Anchor every personal fact in a population statistic so the personal becomes synecdoche. Use the past tense for personal facts and the structural present for analysis; this grammar separates witness from argument. Refuse the redemption arc — the "I escaped" narrative is exactly the meritocratic story Liu, Shaheen and Giridharadas explicitly reject. Credit chance and structure visibly ("I got lucky with one good teacher"; "tuition fees were lower when I started"). Never describe yourself as inspiring, and don't let interviewers do it without correcting them.

**Caroline Criado Perez's calibration is the closest template to the brief**: "rage simmering beneath the surface, but the force comes from the steady, unrelenting accumulation of evidence." The polemic isn't suppressed — it's load-bearing under the data. The reader feels it; the author almost never names it.

### Avoiding the techno-solutionism trap

Morozov's diagnosis is that solutionism doesn't fail by being ineffective — it fails by *redescribing* the problem so the underlying political conflict disappears. Obesity becomes "people lack the right nudge app" rather than "food regulation"; inequality becomes "people lack financial literacy tools" rather than "wages, tax, housing." The phrases that should be permanently banned from any post or pitch are: "disrupting inequality," "democratising X," "AI for good" or "tech for good" without specifics, "data will save us," "empowering," "giving voice to," "solving inequality/poverty," "bridging the digital divide" without a redistribution mechanism, "scaling impact," "game-changer," "revolutionising," "transforming lives." The cautionary case studies to internalise are One Laptop Per Child (Morgan Ames's *The Charisma Machine* is the definitive postmortem), blockchain-for-poverty projects, algorithmic welfare systems (Robodebt, the Dutch SyRI scandal, UK Universal Credit's automated decisions — these are direct trading-systems-adjacent warnings), and microfinance's debt-trap legacy in Bangladesh and India.

The positive frame is Cegłowski's: technologists "hold technology politically accountable for its promises." Usable phrasings include: "Engineers don't get to write the rules. We get to build with the rules we're given — which is why the rules matter." "Code is downstream of policy. I want to be useful upstream." "I have one specific skill — turning unstructured data into something legible. That's a contribution, not a solution." **The single most important move is to name prior workers in every piece**: tenants' unions, IFS, Resolution Foundation, IPPR, NEF, Trust for London, Joseph Rowntree, ONS researchers, specific journalists. Crediting forward is a citation practice, not a rhetorical flourish.

### Handling attacks from both sides

The "you're an engineer, you don't understand economics" critique is defused by visible literacy in the field — citing Piketty, Atkinson, Saez/Zucman, Deaton, Mazzucato, Stiglitz, Diane Coyle, and heterodox voices like Blakeley, Meadway, Mazzucato, Kelton — plus conceding the technical limit and rotating to mechanism: "I'm not an economist. I'm an engineer who builds models on data about how people are paid, taxed, evicted, hired. Here is what the data shows about X. Here is who I asked when I didn't understand Y." Making the method visible (code, data sources, choice points) closes the loop. James Plunkett's "I've been a UBI sceptic; I've moved to cautious supporter in principle" register — visible updating in public — disarms the naïveté charge better than any defensive move.

The "you're complicit working in trading systems" critique is the more dangerous attack and the one to plan for. The Liu/Giridharadas move is to **concede the complicity, don't dodge**. Pretending you're outside the system you critique destroys credibility faster than acknowledging you're inside it. Acknowledge the day job explicitly in the bio; distinguish between the skill (portable), the salary (funds the work), and the project (separate); never claim to be "reforming finance from within" — that's the discredited move Giridharadas's whole book lampoons. The steelman-plus-concrete-answer disarms the strongest critic: "The strongest version of the critique is that every hour I build trading systems is an hour the financial sector gets more efficient at extracting value. I take that seriously. Here's what I do about it: [donate to X, contribute open-source work to Y, give time to Z]." When attacked from both sides, build something concrete that demonstrates the third way — this is Timnit Gebru's DAIR move, and structurally what the openpoverty-uk tool is for here.

### Bio templates that thread the needle

A short LinkedIn bio that works: *"Software engineer based in London. I build trading systems at a fintech startup; previously at GE Digital. My published AI research (Springer, SGAI-AI 2025) focuses on [your specific area]. Outside the day job, I work on economic justice and widening participation in tech — using the same engineering toolkit on questions of inequality, housing, and access. Always interested in talking to researchers, organisers, journalists, and policy people working on the same questions."*

A longer About page in Plunkett/Shaheen register: *"I'm a software engineer in London. I grew up without the family-and-network shortcuts the tech industry quietly runs on; I learned to code anyway. I worked at GE Digital, then moved to a fintech startup where I build trading systems. My AI research has been published by Springer (SGAI-AI 2025). The day job is honest about what it is: it pays for my time, and the skill is portable. The other half of my work — and increasingly the half I care about most — is turning the same engineering toolkit on the data that describes who the economy works for and who it doesn't. I'm not here to claim engineering will fix any of that. The real work is done by organisers, tenants' unions, journalists, and the policy researchers at places like IFS, Resolution Foundation, NEF, Joseph Rowntree, Trust for London. I want to make my technical contribution to that ecosystem."*

The reading list to absorb before publishing your first essay: Eubanks *Automating Inequality*, O'Neil *Weapons of Math Destruction*, Liu *Abolish Silicon Valley*, Shaheen *Know Your Place*, Plunkett *End State*, Merchant *Blood in the Machine*, Criado Perez *Invisible Women*, Giridharadas *Winners Take All*, Morozov *To Save Everything, Click Here*. The user is well-positioned to write the missing British engineer-turning-critic book — Liu's UK cousin — but only after demonstrating the lineage has been absorbed.

## What this rebrand is really doing

The strategy laid out above is not "build a personal brand." It's the deliberate construction of a **bridge identity** — a figure credentialed enough in engineering that the polemic is treated as informed, and clear-eyed enough about engineering's limits that the politics is treated as serious. Wendy Liu, James Plunkett, Faiza Shaheen, Cathy O'Neil and Maciej Cegłowski all occupy versions of this position. None of them got there by trying to sound like an economist. They got there by writing as exactly what they were — an ex-founder, a Downing Street adviser, a working-class researcher, a hedge-fund quant, a startup CEO — and using that vantage as the warrant for the structural argument.

The biggest risk is not under-performance; it's drift. Three drift patterns to actively defend against: pigeonholing as "the AI ethics guy" because the SGAI paper is the loudest credential (counter with three balanced content pillars from week one); getting pulled into party-tribal X discourse because it juices engagement (counter with the Criado Perez calibration — rage beneath, evidence on top); and using the day job as either a hidden secret or a redemption arc (counter with the Liu disclosure — name it, get it out of the way, move on).

The biggest leverage is also not obvious from the calendar. It's the **GitHub-repo-per-post discipline**. That single habit is what separates this rebrand from the hundreds of similar attempts that read as opinion. Reproducibility is the moat. Everything else is downstream.
